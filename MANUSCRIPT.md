# Introduction  {#introduction}

*“You can’t go back and change the beginning, but you can start where you are and change the ending.”* –C.S. Lewis

### **The Phone Call That Rewrote My Life**

I never planned on becoming a financial advisor.

My path was medicine. I was studying to become an orthopedic surgeon, and I loved the idea of helping people heal. I grew up racing dirt bikes and had my fair share of injuries, so orthopedics always felt like a natural fit for me.

Then one day, my life took a turn I never expected.

My dad, Len, passed away suddenly and unexpectedly at 49 years old while I was serving a two-year mission for my church. In one moment, everything changed for my family.

Dad was a custom home builder in Canada. He was hardworking, visionary, and all-in for our family. He carried the financial leadership in our home: taxes, business decisions, insurance, and income strategy. My mom, Susie, trusted him with their finances completely, the way many spouses do. They worked as a team in life, but like so many couples, one person held most of the financial details in their head.

When he passed, we realized just how much he had quietly been carrying for all of us financially and was now on us to figure out how to put together the pieces of the financial puzzle.

To make matters harder, the housing market in Canada was under serious pressure at the time. Investors had pulled out of his projects. Obligations were still there. Contractors still needed to be paid. Bills still needed to be covered. But now the person who had managed all of it was gone without notice and way to early in life.

My mom was grieving the loss of her husband and trying to untangle legal and financial complexity at the exact same time.

I still remember contractors and claimants coming to her saying things such as, "Susie, if we can settle this now, there won’t be any need for court." She didn’t know what was legitimate and what wasn’t. She didn’t know which documents mattered most. She didn’t know who to trust or turn to for advice.

Ultimately, my Mom and Dad’s estate had to go through probate, which is expensive, public, and painfully slow—exactly the opposite of what your family needs during a difficult time. This isn’t uncommon. Most people don’t expect to pass away so early in life, and because of that, many families are not properly set up for that kind of sudden transition.

That was our reality.

### **The Banker Boxes on My Mom’s Floor**

When I got home from my mission, my mom asked me to help her figure out the confusion she was facing with their finances because I was the oldest child. I didn’t have financial training. At the time, I was pursuing pre-med coursework. But I felt a deep responsibility to do whatever I could.

Then the banker boxes started showing up.

His attorney’s office sent box after box filled with statements, contracts, tax records, and legal paperwork from my dad’s business and personal finances. My job became trying to make sense of it all so my mom could move forward and heal from the sadness that came from the loss of her best friend and husband.

The more I dug in, the more I saw a painful truth.

My parents had worked incredibly hard to build a business to provide a great life for my sisters and I. They were good people. They weren’t financially reckless. But what looked like “a plan” from the outside was really a portfolio.

There were accounts, but no integrated framework. There were documents, but no simple process my mom could follow.

At the exact moment she needed clarity most, she got complexity.

She was only 47\. Widowed too soon, suddenly responsible for everything, and unsure how to move forward. Watching my mom walk through that storm changed the way I looked at life—and especially the way I thought about money and security. Seeing her face our new reality shaped me forever. She was brave and strong, but often unsure and worried.  I credit a lot to her bravery... but also now saw how unnecessary this all was.  But hindsight is always 20/20.

### **Where My Calling Changed**

My “aha moment” came in the anatomy lab while I was working on a cadaver.

I clearly remember feeling that  internal shift, almost like two paths in front of me. On one path, I could keep going toward surgery and help people heal physically. On the other, I could step into the financial world and help families avoid the kind of pain and confusion my mom was carrying. I felt drawn to the latter and in that instant, my purpose came into focus.

I realized I actually geeked out on numbers, which surprised me almost as much as anyone else. But that realization came with a promise I made to myself: what my parents went through would never happen to my own family, and if I could help it, it wouldn’t happen to anyone I loved or cared about either.

I felt called to my new mission in life.

I’m a big believer that when God closes a door, He opens another. For me, this was that moment. I stepped from what was familiar into what was uncertain, and with faith I changed direction from medicine to finance.

It wasn’t easy. It took years of study, training, and hard-earned experience. But over time, that new path became my life’s work.

Today, I’m blessed and grateful to serve families throughout the United States through Capital Wealth, the firm I founded, where my team and I serve as fiduciaries. I also have the privilege of sharing these conversations on radio, TV, and in the media, reaching families who may never walk into my office but still need clarity and direction.

At its core, my work is still about healing—just in a different way. Instead of setting bones, I help mend financial uncertainty. Instead of surgical plans, we build written retirement plans designed to protect families when life shifts unexpectedly. The kind of steady guidance I once wished my own parents had when they needed it most.

### **Why Good Savers Still Feel Exposed**

I’ve sat with couples and individuals who had done everything right and still felt uncertain about what came next. I’ve met widows, like my mom, holding stacks of paperwork and searching for a starting point. I’ve worked with retirees who had folders full of statements yet still wondered, “How much reliable monthly income do we actually have?”

Most believed they had a retirement plan. But when we sat down and looked at it together, what they usually had was a portfolio, not a plan.

A portfolio matters. It’s an important piece of the picture. But by itself, it doesn’t answer the questions that bring real peace, clarity, and confidence:

How will income show up each month? Which accounts should be used first—and why? How will taxes be managed over decades, not just this year? What happens if healthcare needs increase? How will inflation affect purchasing power over time? How will a surviving spouse move forward with confidence? And how will assets transfer to the next generation with order instead of confusion?

A portfolio is a list of ingredients. A plan is the recipe.

Without thoughtful coordination, even strong assets can still leave a family feeling uncertain.

# Chapter 1: Your Retirement, Your Terms: What You Do Next Changes Everything {#chapter-1:-your-retirement,-your-terms:-what-you-do-next-changes-everything}

*“It is in your moments of decision that your destiny is shaped.”* –Tony Robbins

I love that Tony Robbins quote. Did you take it in? It hits deep. That is especially true in retirement.

Retirement is not shaped by a single decision, but by a series of them. Decisions about income. Decisions about taxes. Decisions about investments, healthcare, timing, and how all the pieces will work together over time. Those decisions shape whether retirement feels like freedom and confidence or stress and uncertainty.

Most people spend thirty or forty years building assets. Far fewer spend enough time deciding how those assets will support the life they want to live once the paycheck stops. That is where the deeper questions begin. Where will dependable income come from? How will you respond when taxes rise, markets shift, or healthcare needs change? What will life look like not just in the first few honeymoon years of retirement, but in the years that follow?

Real clarity does not come from having more accounts, more statements, or more financial products. It comes from having a written plan that shows how the pieces fit together and what each piece is meant to do. Without that kind of coordination, even hardworking and responsible people can feel unsettled. The issue is not a lack of effort. The issue is a lack of structure. They have the ingredients, but not the recipe.

I saw that in a deeply personal way in my own family. My dad carried much of the financial picture in his head: the accounts, the decisions, the moving parts, and how everything connected. My mom was not incapable, uninvolved, or unaware. She was a strong and intelligent woman facing what many spouses face when one person has naturally taken the lead in holding the financial details together. When my dad passed, what had seemed organized from the outside became incredibly difficult to navigate. The money may have been there, but the clarity was not. And in a moment already made heavy by grief, that lack of clarity made everything even harder.

That experience shaped the way I think about retirement planning to this day. A good plan should not live only in one person’s head. It should be clear enough to explain at the kitchen table, simple enough to follow, and strong enough to hold up when life takes an unexpected turn. Think about the difference between having a recipe written out step by step in one place and trying to piece a meal together from memory, loose notes, and three different cookbooks. That is the difference between having financial pieces and having a real plan.

So if some of this feels messy right now, take a breath. That does not mean you have failed. It means you are human. And it means you are in exactly the right place to begin creating clarity.

## **Why “A Portfolio” Still Leaves People Anxious**

A portfolio is a collection of financial tools. A plan is a coordinated system that directs each tool what job to do, when to do it, and how to adjust when conditions change. Most people have portfolios. Far fewer have a real plan for turning those assets into a lasting retirement

A portfolio might include 401(k)s and IRAs, pensions, taxable brokerage accounts, Social Security estimates, maybe an annuity, insurance policies, and a spreadsheet with projected returns. None of that is bad. But none of that automatically answers where monthly income comes from, how to avoid tax surprises, how to reduce sequence-of-return damage, how to plan for healthcare and long-term care, how to protect a surviving spouse, or how to transfer legacy without creating chaos.

That is why we say at Capital Wealth so often: **a portfolio is not a plan.**

What many people have is what I think of as the kitchen-drawer approach to retirement.

ou probably have one of those drawers at home. I think we all do, the one filled with gadgets, mystery keys, loose batteries, old chargers, and a pen that may or may not work. There are useful things in it, no doubt. But without organization, it becomes frustrating when you need something quickly. 

Many retirement situations look exactly the same. There are accounts, policies, statements, and scattered pieces of information, but no clear structure showing how they are meant to work together when it matters most.

In many cases, the problem is not that people failed to prepare, but that no one ever helped them turn those pieces into a clear and coordinated plan.  People did what they were told for years: save, invest, accumulate. Now they have plenty of pieces, but no obvious order. No withdrawal roadmap. No tax strategy. No guardrails. No clear process for both spouses. 

And I’m going to come right out and say it: *if both spouses can’t run it, it’s not done.* 

A portfolio can live in one spouse’s head. A real plan should survive either spouse. Both partners should be able to explain where income comes from, where key documents are, who to call, and what happens first if one spouse passes. If both spouses cannot run the plan, the plan is not finished.

And this lack of clarity is what tends to leave people feeling anxious.

But, we’ve seen it time and time again. Once you take your portfolio and plug it into a written, strategic plan, the “aha moment” happens and the weight begins to lift.

### **When the Questions Change**

Retirement is a major life transition, and it catches many people off guard in ways they never expected. During your career, the strategy was mostly clear: earn income, save consistently, invest for growth, repeat. That is the “accumulation phase”. 

Retirement is the “distribution phase”, and it requires a different skill set. 

Now the questions become more complex: How much can we safely spend? Which account should we use first? How do we manage taxes over decades? How do we protect against early market losses? How do we fund healthcare realistically? How do we adapt when life changes?

If you feel like no one really taught you how to answer these questions, you’re right. It isn’t taught in school, it isn’t usually taught at work, and it is rarely taught in a way that coordinates every moving piece. That’s why many smart, disciplined savers still feel uncertain once retirement gets close.

It’s also why retirees can often fall into two traps. The first is underspending out of fear: they can afford meaningful life experiences but stay in defense mode because they don’t trust the plan. The second is overspending without structure: they assume the portfolio alone will carry them and later get squeezed by taxes, volatility, or healthcare costs. Both problems come from the same source: a lack of coordinated, intentional planning.

It’s not just about having money saved, it’s about knowing how to use it wisely, in a way that supports an enjoyable lifestyle and the retirement you've always dreamed of.

So, if you feel late to the party, maybe a little overwhelmed or behind where you think you should be, give yourself some grace. That’s not a failure—it’s a signal. No one taught you this part, but you’re here now. And once the pieces of your plan come together, you might just be pleasantly surprised, or at the very least you’ll know what to do next.

There is an old proverb that says the best time to plant a tree was twenty years ago. The second-best time is today. That is a good reminder in retirement planning too. You do not have to fix everything all at once. You just need to begin taking the right steps.

### **You Made It to the Top, Descend Mindfully**

Our firm’s logo is a simple mountain design inspired by the Wasatch Range I see outside my Utah office. It’s more than just an image; it’s a symbol of your financial life.Think of retirement like a mountain expedition. During your working years, you are climbing the mountain. That is the accumulation phase: saving, investing, and building wealth. Reaching the summit, retirement, feels like a big victory. And it is. But the journey does not stop there.

In fact, there is a little-known truth from the world of mountaineering: many accidents happen on the way down, not on the way up. The danger often comes after the summit, when fatigue sets in, conditions change, and people assume the hardest part is behind them. A bottle of water and a protein bar may be enough for a casual hike, but a serious mountain ascent requires far more than that. It requires preparation, awareness, and a plan for the full journey, including how to descend safely, not just the climb to the top.

Even the most experienced mountaineers do not assume they should go it alone. On the biggest ascents in the world, including Everest, highly skilled climbers still rely on Sherpas and guides. Not because they are weak or incapable, but because the stakes are high, the terrain can change quickly, and experience matters. The higher the mountain, the more valuable wise guidance becomes.

Retirement works much the same way. It is the distribution phase of your financial life, where timing matters and mistakes can be costly. A withdrawal taken in the wrong year can drive taxes higher. A market downturn early in retirement can do outsized damage. A Medicare or healthcare decision can create consequences that last for years.

That is the difference between an accumulation mindset and a true retirement income strategy. A strong retirement plan accounts for both. It serves as your guide for the descent, helping you avoid unnecessary missteps so you can descend safely and not find yourself stranded at the very moment you had hoped to finally enjoy the view.

## **Five Common Retirement Beliefs to Rethink Right Now** 

The retirees who feel calm and confident are rarely following retirement on autopilot. They have usually moved beyond generic rules of thumb and started thinking more intentionally about how their plan actually works in real life. We will break each of these ideas down in much greater detail in the chapters ahead, but I want to begin with a few practical wins you can apply immediately. These are five common retirement beliefs that are worth rethinking now.

#### **Common belief \#1:  *“When we hit $1 million, we’re set.”***

**What often happens**   
A million dollars can be more than enough for one household and not nearly enough for another. A couple may hit their “magic number” and feel secure at first, only to find that rising grocery costs, a surprise roof repair, higher property taxes, and healthcare expenses create more pressure than they expected. What once felt like a safe number can start to feel a lot less certain.

**A stronger approach**  
Start with real life, not an undefined number. Calculate your must-have income and your want-to-have lifestyle spending. Map your predictable income sources and plan for the unexpected. Retirement outcomes are shaped not just by a balance, but by how well income, taxes, healthcare, investment returns, inflation, and legacy planning work 

#### **Common belief \#2: *“Withdrawing 4% per year will cover us.”***

**What often happens**  
Two neighbors withdraw the same percentage from similar portfolios. One retires into a relatively calm market and does fine. The other hits a rough first few years, sells investments at lower values to create income, and never fully recovers. Same withdrawal rate. Very different outcome. 

**A stronger approach**  
Don’t anchor your retirement to a single percentage. A withdrawal strategy should reflect your actual spending needs, your income sources, your tax picture, and the order in which market returns occur. The more flexible and coordinated the plan, the more resilient it tends to be when life and markets change**.**

#### **Common belief \#3: *“Taxes will be lower in retirement.”***

**What often happens**  
Retirement income can come from more places than people expect, and much of it can still be taxable. Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs), taxes on Social Security, Medicare premium surcharges called Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount (IRMAA), and a surviving spouse filing as single can all raise the tax burden over time.

**A stronger approach**  
Plan taxes forward, not backward. Coordinate withdrawals across account types, consider strategic Roth conversions in the right years, and time Social Security in the context of your full income picture. Good tax planning is not just about this year’s return. It is about reducing lifetime tax drag.

#### **Common belief \#4: *“Medicare covers it”***

**What often happens**  
Many retirees are surprised to learn that Medicare does not cover everything. Premiums, deductibles, copays, prescription costs, dental, vision, hearing, and especially long-term care can create far more out-of-pocket expense than people expect. Without a plan, healthcare costs can start crowding out travel, generosity, and other parts of retirement people were looking forward to. 

**A stronger approach**  
Medicare is foundational, not comprehensive. Healthcare and long-term care need their own place in the plan. Decide in advance how you want to cover routine costs, potential care needs, and larger health events so they do not derail the rest of your retirement. 

#### **Common belief \#5: *“Set it and forget it”*** 

**What often happens**  
Life changes. Markets move. Tax laws change. Health needs shift. What felt like a solid plan at retirement can slowly drift out of alignment over time. Without regular review, small issues can turn into larger and more expensive problems. 

**A stronger approach**  
A retirement plan should be a living system, not a one-time event. Regular strategic reviews help you adjust for changes, stay aligned with your goals, and spot opportunities before they are missed**.**

Confidence does not come from a magic number, a withdrawal rule, or a product. It comes from coordination. When income, taxes, healthcare, investments, inflation, and legacy planning are working together, retirement starts to feel clearer and more secure. That is where the Retirement Money Map helps most: it brings scattered decisions into one coordinated view.

### **What We Mean by Guardrails**

**Guardrails keep you on the road—especially when the weather changes.**  
In retirement, they’re pre-decided rules you set in advance that tell you *when* to adjust—so you’re not guessing in the moment. They define what you will do before pressure arrives and reduce emotional reactions when uncertainty hits.

Common guardrails include an essentials spending floor, a discretionary spending range, a normal-year withdrawal order, a down-market withdrawal order, and tax-bracket ceilings.

**What Guardrails look like in practice:**

* Define your *comfort lane* (your normal spending).

* Set a *lower guardrail* (a “slow down” marker) and an *upper guardrail* (a “you can lean in a bit” marker). Your monthly minimum spend is your floor (core living expenses fully covered), and your maximum spend is your ceiling (floor plus lifestyle spending).

* If markets dip or inflation bites and you touch the lower guardrail, *temporarily* trim discretionary spending (not essentials) or pull from protected/cash reserves.

* If things run strong and you hit the upper guardrail, allow a little more for travel, gifts, or projects—on purpose, not impulse.  
  Here is a simple example: if monthly target income is $8,000, essentials might be $6,400 and discretionary spending might be $1,600. In strong years, spend near full range. In rough years, keep essentials funded and flex discretionary spending intentionally. 

  Guardrails replace uncertainty and panic with *structure and peace*. You don’t have to watch the market every day. Essentials are locked in, lifestyle flexes within your comfort lane, and the map tells you exactly how to pivot without second-guessing.

## **The Big Question: Where Will Your Paycheck Come From?**

Most retirees draw from a mix of sources: Social Security, pensions, annuities, dividends, bond interest, and savings withdrawals. Each has its role. The art is blending them into a system that fits your needs, your temperament, and your timeline.

### **Why Blending Beats Betting**

You’ve heard “don’t put all your eggs in one basket.” Same idea here—but with higher stakes. In retirement, blending your income sources strategically usually beats betting on any single stream to carry everything. Here’s why:

**1\) Market volatility is inevitable.**  
If the market dips and you’re relying solely on investments for income, each withdrawal can lock in losses and leave fewer shares available to recover. That is what makes sequence-of-returns risk so damaging, and we’ll unpack it more in the next section. A wiser approach is to blend income sources so you are not forced to sell at the worst possible time. The goal is to keep a difficult market from spilling over into daily life and disrupting the moments retirement is meant to make possible.

**2\) Life isn’t linear.**  
Spending shifts through the Go-Go, Slow-Go, and No-Go years. A blend lets you enjoy making memories early and still stay secure later—permission to take the trip now.

**3\) Inflation quietly erodes.**

Inflation rarely announces itself all at once. It shows up quietly through higher grocery bills, rising insurance premiums, more expensive travel, and the steady increase in the everyday costs of living. Over time, that quiet drift can erode buying power more than many retirees expect. A thoughtful income plan accounts for that reality from the beginning, giving you a dependable foundation today while preserving flexibility for the years ahead.

**4\) Healthcare costs rise**  
Healthcare costs tend to rise differently and often faster than many other expenses in retirement. Premiums, copays, prescriptions, dental work, vision needs, and long-term care can all place increasing pressure on income over time. That is why a well-built plan needs more than a fixed paycheck. It needs room to adjust. The right blend of income sources can provide steady support for essentials while giving you the flexibility to handle rising healthcare costs without letting them crowd out the people, moments, and experiences that matter most. 

**4\) Taxes change the net number.**  
Which account you draw from, and when, matters more than most people realize. Thoughtful, tax-smart withdrawals can help you keep more of your income, reduce unnecessary tax drag, and create more flexibility over time. We’ll unpack that in much greater detail in Chapter 3\. 

**5\) Survivor income can step down.**  
When one spouse passes away, income often changes immediately. Social Security usually drops to the higher single benefit, and some pensions may be reduced or end altogether. A blended plan accounts for that shift in advance so the surviving spouse can maintain the lifestyle the plan was built to support.

### **Survivor Income (Plan This Upfront)**

Social Security usually steps down to the higher single benefit, and some pensions may be reduced or end altogether. But many of the household expenses do not disappear with them.

The goal is to make sure the surviving spouse can still cover the essentials: housing, utilities, groceries, healthcare, and the ongoing costs of daily life, without being forced to sell investments at the wrong time or make major financial decisions in the middle of grief.

A few deliberate choices now can protect dignity, preserve independence, and reduce stress later.

### **The Risk Most People Miss: Sequence of Returns**

Most people believe the average return is what matters most in retirement.

But once you begin withdrawing from your portfolio, when the returns occur matters just as much as what the average is.

#### **What This Means in Plain English**

When you’re taking income from your investments, early losses can do permanent and sometimes significant damage to the longevity of the plan.

If the market declines in the first few years of retirement and you are forced to sell investments for income, you can end up locking in losses. You have to sell more shares at lower prices to generate the same income, which reduces the number of shares left in the portfolio. Then, when the market eventually recovers, fewer shares remain to participate in the rebound. 

Even if long-term average returns are identical, the outcome can be dramatically different.

* Treat the Market Growth layer as opportunistic, not obligatory. Let it grow; tap it when conditions are favorable.

## **It’s Not What You Make. It’s What You Keep**

One of the best lessons I ever got came from my dad. He used to say, “It’s not how much you make—it’s how much you keep that matters.” And in retirement, those words couldn’t be more true. I still think about that often, and I’m grateful for the wisdom he passed down to me.

But if you can recognize that same income earlier, in a year when you’re still in the 12% bracket, the tax may be closer to $12,000, leaving you with about $88,000. That’s a $10,000 difference on the same $100,000.

That is what I mean by a 10% swing. You didn’t earn it by taking more investment risk, chasing a hot stock, or hoping the market gives you an extra return. You created it by being intentional about when income shows up on your tax return. In retirement, that kind of planning matters. Keeping yourself in a lower bracket when possible can mean more money for travel, generosity, healthcare, or simply more breathing room. It’s one of the few ways to materially improve what you keep without increasing the risk you take.

## **The Tax Time Bomb**

You did the disciplined thing for decades: saved into your 401(k) or IRA, took the tax break, and let it grow. These are tax-deferred accounts, which simply means you have not paid taxes on that money yet. The taxes are delayed, not erased. 401(k)s and traditional IRAs are only the most familiar examples. Other common tax-deferred accounts include 403(b)s, 457 plans, Thrift Savings Plans (TSPs), SEP IRAs, and SIMPLE IRAs. These accounts, often referred to by the IRS as tax-deferred accounts, may have been built through payroll deductions, employer matches, and years of market growth. That wasn’t a mistake. It was smart. But here’s the part most people aren’t told: tax-deferred does not mean tax-free. It means taxed later. In other words, a portion of that balance is really an IOU to the IRS.

And later eventually arrives.

Generally, at a certain age, Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) begin. Under current law, that age is generally 73 for many retirees, with age 75 applying to some people born later. A later RMD age might sound beneficial at first, since it gives the account more time before withdrawals are required. But that delay is not always an advantage. In some cases, it may compress future withdrawals into a shorter time frame, which could lead to larger required distributions later and potentially higher taxes, greater Social Security taxation, or higher Medicare premiums.”

RMDs are *forced income*—the IRS requires you take out a calculated amount every year, whether you need the money or not. Most dollars come out as ordinary income, except any after-tax basis in the account.

That’s where the “time bomb” shows up—because one trigger sets off the next:

* **Tax bracket creep.** Forced withdrawals can push you into a higher tax bracket—even in years you didn’t plan to spend much.

* **Social Security taxation.** Higher reported income can make more of your Social Security taxable.

* **Medicare surcharges (IRMAA).** Cross certain income lines and next year’s Medicare premiums may jump—often a surprise, thanks to the two-year lookback rule.

* **The widow’s penalty.** When one spouse passes, the survivor will often face a much less favorable tax picture. After the year of death, the surviving spouse may have to file as single, which can mean narrower tax brackets, a lower standard deduction, and potentially higher taxes on the same or even less income.

* **Heirs and the 10-year rule.** Adult children who inherit pre-tax accounts generally must empty them within 10 years—often during their own peak earning years—creating a tax mess you never intended.

It’s not just math; it’s *life*. A bigger-than-expected tax bill can mean canceling a trip, delaying a gift to the grandkids, or feeling that low-grade anxiety every time you open the mail.

No one wants their children to receive what was meant to be a meaningful inheritance, only to discover it comes with a significant tax burden attached. Most parents hope to leave behind a blessing, not a complication. With a little planning, more of what you pass on may go to your family and less may be lost to taxes. 

Here’s the key mindset: doing nothing is still a decision. If you let everything ride in tax-deferred accounts until RMDs arrive, you’re handing the steering wheel to the IRS. They’ll decide *when* income shows up on your return and how it ripples through your plan.

When you’re required to pull money during a down market to satisfy taxes or an RMD, you’re not only withdrawing—you’re locking in losses. That’s the same “sequence of returns” risk we talked about in Chapter 2, now wearing a tax badge.

The good news? You have options before any of this becomes unavoidable. You can choose when to recognize income, which accounts to draw from, and how to smooth your brackets so you’re not triggering hidden tripwires (Social Security taxation, IRMAA, and more). Think of it as carefully defusing the tax time bomb: one wire at a time, on your timetable, with a clear plan.

In the pages ahead, we’ll walk through how to do exactly that—so more of your money funds *your* life, not avoidable taxes.

### **RMDs in Real Life**

Each year, the IRS determines your Required Minimum Distribution using the prior December 31 balance of each applicable tax-deferred retirement account and a life-expectancy factor from the appropriate IRS table. But the rules are more detailed than many people realize. It is not always as simple as adding every account together and dividing once. In many cases, IRAs can be aggregated for withdrawal purposes, while 401(k) and 457(b) plan RMDs generally must be handled separately. The key point is simple: the IRS requires a minimum amount to come out, but the exact calculation depends on the type of account. 

Then it was Ron’s turn. His birthday gift from the IRS arrived in the mail: it was time to take his first Required Minimum Distribution. He didn’t need the extra income that year, but the rule wasn’t optional. Ron had about $453,000 in his traditional IRA as of December 31 of the prior year. Using the IRS Uniform Lifetime Table for his age, that balance resulted in a first IRA RMD of roughly $17,100. And every dollar of that distribution counted as ordinary income on his tax return.

But that wasn’t the whole story. Ron also had an old 401(k), and that account had its own separate RMD requirement. So he couldn’t just look at the IRA number, take that amount, and move on. More money would also have to come out of the 401(k), whether he needed it or not. That extra forced income could push him into a higher marginal tax bracket and create a ripple effect throughout the rest of his plan, including making more of his Social Security taxable and increasing his Medicare premiums.

If Ron missed one of those required withdrawals, the IRS could make a frustrating situation even worse. Under current law, the penalty can be 25% of the amount that should have been taken, though it may be reduced to 10% if the mistake is corrected promptly. He would still generally owe ordinary income tax on the distribution too. That’s why it’s smart to put these dates on the calendar and automate as much as possible. The last thing you want is for a rule you could have planned for to create an unnecessary headache.

There’s one more wrinkle here, and it’s where plenty of people get tripped up trying to handle RMDs on their own. A lot of folks assume the first RMD deadline is April 15, because that date is burned into our brains at tax time. But the first RMD is actually due by April 1 of the following year. Yes, April Fools’ Day. You do have to wonder whether Congress had a sense of humor when they picked that one. The catch is that delaying the first withdrawal may not help as much as people think. In some cases, it means taking two RMDs in the same tax year, because after that first one, every future RMD is due by December 31\. That can push taxable income even higher. Once RMDs begin, the IRS expects its share every year.

### **The Dream of Lower Taxes in Retirement**

When tax-deferred accounts first became widespread, the popular wisdom was that you’d be in a lower tax bracket during retirement. After all, you’d no longer have a salary. Makes sense, right?

Unfortunately, reality often plays out differently.

Many people today want to maintain their lifestyle—or even improve it—during retirement. They want to travel, spend time with family, pursue hobbies, and give generously. That requires income. And the more income you draw from tax-deferred sources, the higher your taxable income becomes.

We frequently see retirees ending up in the same tax bracket they had during their working years, and sometimes in an even higher one. Why? Because while a paycheck may stop, taxable income often does not. Social Security, pensions, investment income, and Required Minimum Distributions can stack together quickly. At the same time, many retirees lose deductions they once relied on and can no longer reduce taxable income through workplace retirement plan contributions. For widows and widowers, the shift to single-filer tax brackets can make the situation even worse. So while retirement may feel like a lower-income season, it does not always translate into a lower tax bracket.

## **Understanding the Three Tax Buckets (and Why They Change Everything)**

Before we talk tactics, here’s the simple lens I use with every family: the same dollar can be taxed three different ways. When you sort your assets into these three buckets, taxation finally starts to make sense, and the planning becomes powerful.

**1\) Taxable Bucket**

**What lives here:** Brokerage accounts, savings accounts, CDs, money market accounts, and other investments held in after-tax accounts, including stocks, ETFs, mutual funds, and bonds.

**How it’s taxed:** Interest income from savings accounts, CDs, money market accounts, and many bonds is generally taxed at ordinary income tax rates. If you sell an investment for more than you paid for it, the profit is a capital gain. Gains on investments held for more than one year are generally taxed at long-term capital gains rates, which are often more favorable. Gains on investments held for one year or less are generally taxed at short-term capital gains rates, which are taxed like ordinary income.

Ordinary income is taxed at your marginal tax bracket, up to the top federal ordinary income rate under current law. Long-term capital gains are typically taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% federally, depending on your taxable income. If your income rises above certain thresholds, you may also owe the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT), along with any applicable state taxes

**Why it matters:** This bucket offers flexibility. With careful timing of capital gains, tax-loss harvesting, and charitable gifting strategies, you can often reduce or offset taxes. Done correctly, it can be one of the most controllable buckets.

One of the clearest ways this flexibility shows up is through tax-loss harvesting.

**Turning Market Losses into Tax Savings**

Let’s say a client owns two different stocks in a brokerage account. One stock has a $20,000 gain. Another stock is down $8,000. If the stock with the gain is sold on its own, the client may owe tax on the full $20,000 gain. But if the stock with the $8,000 loss is also sold, that loss can offset part of the gain, potentially reducing the taxable amount to $12,000.

That is tax-loss harvesting in simple terms: using investment losses strategically so less of the gain is exposed to taxes.

At first glance, this may seem to go against the usual advice to avoid selling at a low. But this is not panic-selling. It is a deliberate planning move: realizing a loss for tax purposes while keeping the money invested in a way that still supports the long-term plan.

Of course, this must be done carefully. Tax-loss harvesting is subject to wash-sale rules, which means the money needs to be reinvested thoughtfully so the tax benefit is not lost.

What makes this valuable is that it can turn market volatility into a planning opportunity. A disappointing decline in one stock does not always have to be wasted pain. In the right circumstances, that loss can be used to reduce taxes, improve portfolio efficiency, and keep more money working toward the long-term plan.

Done properly, this is not about emotional trading or reacting to headlines. It is a disciplined part of thoughtful wealth management: reviewing taxable accounts carefully, identifying positions that may no longer be the best fit, harvesting losses where appropriate, and reinvesting in a way that keeps the portfolio aligned with the client’s broader goals.

In some cases, more advanced portfolio strategies can create even greater tax-harvesting opportunities, but the core idea is simple: use losses thoughtfully so gains are not taxed more than they need to be.

As I mentioned earlier, it is not just about how much you make. It is about how much you keep. Investors who focus only on rate of return can miss the bigger strategy. When taxes are ignored, part of the return quietly slips away. But when gains, losses, and taxation are managed together, more of the portfolio’s progress stays with the client.

**2\) Tax-Deferred Bucket**

**What lives here:**Traditional IRAs, 401(k)s, TSPs, pensions, and other tax-deferred retirement accounts.

**How it’s taxed:** Withdrawals are taxed at ordinary income tax rates, which are generally the highest rates in the tax code.

**Why it matters:** This bucket often represents the largest pool of retirement savings. But every dollar withdrawn increases taxable income. That can impact Social Security taxation and Medicare premiums. Strategic moves—like Roth conversions, Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs), RMD timing, and tax-bracket management—can help prevent unnecessary tax creep.

**3\) Tax-Free Bucket**

**What lives here:** Roth IRAs, Roth TSPs and Roth 401(k)s, along with Health Savings Accounts (HSAs) for qualified medical expenses.

**How it’s taxed:** When used properly, qualified withdrawals are generally free from federal income tax.

**Why it matters:** This bucket gives you leverage. When used properly, it can provide income without adding to taxable income, which may help avoid higher brackets, greater Social Security taxation, and Medicare IRMAA surcharges. And if tax rates are higher later in retirement, this bucket may become even more valuable because it gives you flexibility when other withdrawals could cost more in taxes. That makes it a powerful tool for withdrawal sequencing and long-term legacy planning.

### **A Quick Side-by-Side: “If I need $100,000…”**

* **From the Taxable Bucket:** You sell $100,000 of investments. Only the gain portion is typically taxable, which often makes this bucket one of the more flexible sources of retirement income.

* **From the Tax-Deferred Bucket:** You withdraw $100,000, and most or all of it is taxed as ordinary income. That can make this bucket more expensive to draw from than many retirees first realize.

* **From the Tax-Free Bucket:** If it comes from a qualified Roth distribution, $100,000 to you may mean $100,000 spendable. Generally, no federal income tax is due, and the withdrawal does not add to taxable income, making this bucket especially useful in later retirement years.

Same $100,000 goal—very different after-tax outcomes. You cannot control future tax law. But you can control which bucket you pull from—and when.

That’s how you turn a pile of savings into a retirement paycheck that feels steady, intentional, and tax-smart.

Tax rates move. Over the decades, top brackets have swung higher and lower, and tax laws occasionally create limited windows where recognizing income costs less than usual. Think of those windows like a sale aisle at your favorite store. When rates are temporarily lower relative to your lifetime, you can “buy back” part of your IRA from the IRS at a discount—through things like bracket-filling withdrawals or Roth conversions.

That’s the opportunity mindset I want you to have. Not fear—readiness. If a season of lower rates shows up, or your own income dips in a given year (retired but before RMDs, a sabbatical, a business sale spread across years), that may be the moment to move pre-tax dollars into tax-free dollars on purpose.

Two ideas to keep in your back pocket:

* \#1: Rates shift, laws sunset. Some years are simply better “prices” for recognizing income than others.

* \#2: Markets wobble. A lower market value can make a Roth conversion more efficient—and future rebounds inside the Roth are yours, tax-free.

You don’t have to time anything perfectly. You just need a strategy mapped out. When a low-tax window opens, your retirement plan should already outline what to convert, how much to fill in your current bracket, and where the tax is paid from. That’s how you turn volatility and tax changes into quiet advantages—so taxes and markets play in the background and enjoying your life stays front and center.

## **Strategies to Reduce Future Taxes** 

Smart tax planning isn’t about loopholes—it’s about timing, structure, and choosing when and how income shows up on your tax return. The goal isn’t just a lower bill this April; it’s lower lifetime taxes especially if taxes should increase in the future. ~~and more peace year after year~~. Here are three core strategies we use every day with clients.

### **Strategy \#1: Bracket Planning & Income Timing**

**Two levers, one goal: keep lifetime taxes lower.**

**Bracket planning** is deciding *how much* income to show on your return each year so you stay inside the most favorable tax bracket.  
**Income timing** is deciding *when* that income shows up—so it doesn’t spill into the wrong year and trigger avoidable taxes or surcharges.

**How it sounds in practice:**

**Bracket planning:**

*  “Let’s draw $X from IRAs this year to stay below the next bracket.”  
*  “We’re going to keep your income just below the Medicare IRMAA threshold.”  
*  “We’ll delay Social Security one more year and use that window to fill your bracket with partial Roth conversions.”  
  **Income timing:**  
*  “Let’s split that large withdrawal: half in December, half in January.”  
*  “We’ll sell part of that fund this year and the rest next year to help avoid a bracket jump.”

**Why it matters:**

Done well, this can keep future RMDs smaller, help reduce the taxation of Social Security, help avoid Medicare IRMAA surcharges, and reduce the risk of “accidental” bracket jumps when markets or life events create extra income.

### **Strategy \#2: Roth Conversions (Your Flexibility Engine)**

* With a traditional IRA, 401(k), or TSP, part of that balance will eventually go to taxes. A Roth conversion moves dollars from a tax-deferred account to a tax-free Roth account. You pay tax on the amount converted now, but once those dollars are in the Roth, future qualified withdrawals can be generally free from federal income tax. Roth IRAs, and designated Roth workplace accounts under current law, are not subject to lifetime RMDs for the original owner, which can help reduce tax pressure later.  
    
  **Why it matters:**  
* Qualified Roth withdrawals generally do not add to taxable income, which may help with Social Security taxation and Medicare IRMAA thresholds.    
* No lifetime RMDs for the original owner under current law.    
* Roth conversions are not subject to income or age limits.    
* A flexible pool of assets for healthcare, travel, or helping family.    
* A potentially valuable legacy asset for heirs, subject to distribution rules.  
    
  **How to do it wisely:**  
* Convert in lower-income years to fill, not overflow, your current bracket.    
* Consider converting when account values are temporarily lower.    
* Watch IRMAA and other tax thresholds.    
* Coordinate with your financial advisor and CPA.    
* Think in terms of a multi-year plan, not a one-time move.  
    
  **Simple example:** 

You have $100,000 in a traditional IRA and convert $30,000 this year. That $30,000 is added to your taxable income now. From then on, that converted amount is in the Roth, where future qualified growth and withdrawals may be generally tax-free. It also reduces the amount left in the tax-deferred account for future RMDs.

Over time, a series of strategic partial conversions can:

* Shrink tax-deferred balances,    
* Reduce future RMDs,    
* Create more flexibility in retirement income planning    
* Build a tax-free bucket for larger needs.

One more reason the Roth matters: what you see is closer to what you keep.

When you look at a Roth IRA statement, the balance is generally a clearer reflection of what may ultimately be available to spend. By contrast, with a traditional IRA, 401(k), or TSP, part of that balance will eventually go to taxes.

## **Organize by Purpose, Not Product**

One of the most powerful ways to protect what you’ve built in retirement is to rethink how you organize your investments. Not by product, account type, or even asset class—but by purpose. This means using each part of your portfolio intentionally, based on what it’s meant to *do*, not just what it is.

The traditional approach many advisors use segment your money by time horizon—short-term, medium-term, and long-term. It’s a decent idea in theory, but in practice, it doesn’t always reflect how people actually live in retirement or what really drives their decisions.

As we explored in Chapter 2, the Retirement Money Map groups your investments the same way we organize your income—by role:

Here’s how that looks:

**Protected Income (safe accounts):** your steady paycheck that doesn’t move down with the market, so the basics are covered month to month.

**Market-Based Growth:** a diversified, long-term engine designed to outpace inflation and fund bigger dreams.

**Emergency Reserves (typically 3–6 months of expenses):** a cash cushion for health costs, surprises, or opportunities—so life’s bumps don’t force bad timing.

Think of it as building a portfolio that behaves more like a well-run household budget. When it’s time to pay the electric bill or buy groceries, you don’t cash out your long-term stock holdings. You use your paycheck. That’s how your investments should work, too—especially in retirement.

### **How the mix gets sized::**

Start by setting guaranteed income and your reserve. Then calibrate with a simple guide:

**The “Rule of 100.”** Subtract your age from 100 to estimate an upper bound for how much could sit in market-based growth; the rest naturally lives in Protected Income and reserves.

* At 60, many land near \~60% Protected Income / \~40% market-based growth (plus a modest cash reserve).  
* At 75, it might be closer to \~75/25.

**Why this helps:** The Rule of 100 is a quick guardrail, not a prediction tool. It gives you an upper limit for market exposure so one bad stretch doesn’t hijack your income plan. In plain terms, it helps you avoid “too much risk by accident,” then tailor from there based on your health, goals, and comfort with risk.

## **What a 401(k) Won’t Do for You (And What Your Plan Must)**

You’ve climbed the mountain of saving—slogging through market dips, job changes, and temptation to spend. Now you’re standing at the summit with a 401(k) that’s taken decades to build. But just as you’re catching your breath, you realize something unsettling: there’s no map for the way down.

A 401(k) is a great savings tool—but it’s not a plan. What this means for you: having a strong balance is only step one. The real benefit comes from turning that balance into a coordinated income, tax, and withdrawal system you can actually live on with confidence.

Here’s what it won’t do for you, and how your plan needs to step in to navigate.

#### **Schedule Withdrawals**

You can tap a 401(k) penalty-free at 59½ (and in some cases earlier, such as certain age-55 separation situations), but it won’t tell you when or how much to pull—or warn you when a distribution tips you into a higher bracket. It also won’t help you balance today’s income need with needs 10 years from now.

**What your plan does:** Sets a withdrawal calendar aligned to your budget and tax bracket targets.

*Example:* Instead of a one-off $20,000 in December, the plan automates monthly transfers and pre-books any lump sums in Q1 so you can model taxes first.

#### **Coordinate taxes**

Every traditional 401(k) dollar comes out as ordinary income. Poor timing can start a domino effect—more of your Social Security becomes taxable and your modified adjusted gross income (MAGI) crosses an IRMAA line, raising next year’s Medicare Part B/D premiums.

**What your plan does:** Paces distributions and uses Roth/taxable buckets to keep MAGI inside guardrails. 

*Example:* Cap IRA withdrawals at your bracket “ceiling,” then finish the month from brokerage/Roth so next year’s Medicare premiums don’t jump.

#### **Create a paycheck**

Balances don’t auto-convert into durable, month-to-month income. 

**What your plan does:** Blends guaranteed sources with scheduled transfers so the plan pays you, not the market.

*Example:* Pension \+ Social Security \+ a set monthly draw from Protected Income replaces the “hope-and-guess 4%.”

#### **Handle RMDs for you**

Required Minimum Distributions arrive whether you need the money or not—and they’re taxable. Your account custodian may process RMDs, but it won’t coordinate the tax impact across all your accounts.

**What your plan does:** Uses earlier, smaller withdrawals and bracket-aware Roth conversions to shrink future RMDs.  

*Example:* Convert up to your target bracket in the “Roth window” years to reduce the mandatory hits and tax spikes later.

#### **Integrate with your other accounts**

A 401(k) won’t coordinate taxable, tax-deferred, and Roth dollars with healthcare and inflation in view. 

**What your plan does:** Assigns each account a job—income now, income later, inflation hedge, tax flexibility, legacy.

*Example:* Years 1–3: spend from taxable (harvest gains/losses intentionally) while converting IRA→Roth to your bracket top; use Roth for big one-offs to avoid MAGI spikes (Medicare’s two-year look-back).

**Bottom line:** Your 401(k) can grow money; it can’t run money. The Retirement Money Map supplies the steering wheel, brakes, and dashboard—so the accounts you’ve built finally work together, on purpose.

### **Why “4%” Is a Starting Estimate, Not a Retirement Plan**

Society seems to have painted retirement success as being about hitting a savings number. Treating that magic “retirement number” like it’s a finish line, and then relying on the old 4% rule to carry you home.

For years, retirees were told to follow the “4% rule”—the idea that if you withdraw 4% of your portfolio each year, you’ll have a good chance of not running out of money over a 30-year retirement.

Sounds simple. But simple doesn’t mean reliable.

Rules of thumb can be useful for rough planning. They become dangerous when treated as a full strategy. 

The 4% rule came from historical U.S. market backtests built on past stock-and-bond return periods, which may not match future conditions. It was built for a different economic world than the one you’re retiring into. 

It gives a starting estimate, but it cannot answer the decisions that actually determine retirement outcomes:

Which account should fund this year’s withdrawals?

How do we avoid avoidable tax spikes?

What changes if markets drop in the first two years?

How do we adjust for rising healthcare and inflation over decades?

Two households can withdraw the same percentage and get very different outcomes based on sequence, taxes, and spending flexibility.

A real plan does more than pick a percentage. It assigns roles to accounts, sets withdrawal guardrails, and defines what to do in good markets, bad markets, and everything in between.

Bottom line: your savings number matters. Your withdrawal system matters more.

## **The 401(k) Mistakes to Avoid**

If the Retirement Money Map is the structure, these are the cracks that appear when there isn’t one.

A 401(k) can grow efficiently for decades.  
But unmanaged in retirement, it can quietly create tax spikes, forced withdrawals, and unnecessary risk.

Here are the most common mistakes I see — even among disciplined savers — and how a coordinated plan prevents them.

### **1\. Misaligned Investment Allocation**

A common mistake retirees make is sticking with a portfolio that no longer fits their stage of life. 

It's not uncommon to see someone approaching or entering retirement still invested as if they’re 35 years old—heavy in stocks, chasing returns, and underestimating risk.

On the flip side, some people get spooked by market headlines and swing too far the other way. They go ultra-conservative, loading up on cash or bonds, trying to avoid any volatility. That might feel safe in the short term, but over a 20- or 30-year retirement playing it too safe could leave you vulnerable to inflation and make it harder for your money to last.

Your plan should reflect where you are on the journey—whether you’re five years out, newly retired, or in your later years. The mix of growth assets (like stocks) and stable income-producing assets (like bonds or annuities) needs to evolve with you. And this isn't a “set it and forget it” situation—allocations should be reviewed regularly to stay in sync with your needs.

### **2\. Missing the Window for Roth Conversions**

As we covered in Chapter 3, Roth conversions are most powerful during the gap years before Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) begin.

Yet one of the most common 401(k) mistakes I see is leaving a 401(k) completely untouched simply because the income isn’t needed yet.

On the surface, that sounds disciplined. “Leave it alone and let it grow.”

But retirement creates a temporary window of flexibility. In the early years—before RMDs begin—you often have more control over your taxable income than you ever will again. That window can be used to gradually reposition part of your 401(k) into a Roth IRA while staying inside a controlled tax bracket.

The mistake isn’t having a large 401(k).

The mistake is failing to gradually reposition it while you have flexibility and losing the opportunity to shape your future tax picture intentionally.

### **3\. Poorly Timed Withdrawals and Tax Spikes**

A 401(k) is not a checking account.

One of the most common mistakes retirees make is taking large, unplanned withdrawals without understanding the tax ripple effects.

Need money for a renovation? Helping a grandchild with college? Taking a dream trip?

If you pull a lump sum from your 401(k) without modeling the tax impact first, you may unintentionally push yourself into a higher tax bracket. That extra income can increase how much of your Social Security is taxed or raise your Medicare premiums the following year.

The issue isn’t taking money.

It’s taking money without coordination.

Smart withdrawals are planned in advance. They’re spaced out, timed intentionally, and aligned with your broader tax strategy. Without that structure, a manageable expense can turn into an avoidable tax surprise.

### **4\. The RMD Surprise**

Back in the tax chapter, we talked about RMDs as a tax tripwire. Here, they can become an investment-coordination problem too.

If you’ve delayed distributions because you didn’t need the income—or because you wanted to postpone taxes—your 401(k) may have grown significantly by the time RMDs begin. That means the amount you’re required to withdraw could be larger than you expected, especially after strong market years.

Suddenly, your taxable income rises. You may find yourself in a higher bracket. Medicare premiums can increase due to IRMAA surcharges. More of your Social Security may become taxable.

And unlike a voluntary withdrawal, this one isn’t optional.

With earlier planning—gradual withdrawals, strategic positioning, and thoughtful coordination across accounts—you can reduce the future RMD base and avoid being forced into decisions at the wrong time.

RMDs don’t have to feel like a shock.

But without a plan, they often do.

### **5\. Treating the 401(k) as an Island**

If you withdraw from your 401(k) without considering how it fits into the bigger picture, you could be leaving money on the table. Many retirees have multiple accounts—IRAs, Roth IRAs, brokerage accounts, even HSAs—and each comes with its own tax treatment and strategic potential when integrated into a plan.

For example, a retiree might be better off drawing first from a taxable brokerage account to let their 401(k) grow longer. Or they might use the 401(k) strategically in high-income years and lean on Roth funds when income is lower.

The key is coordination. Your 401(k) isn’t the whole plan—it’s just one ingredient. If you only ever touch that one account, you could overpay in taxes and reduce the longevity of your portfolio.

### **6\. Set-It-and-Forget-It Mentality**

Finally, one of the most dangerous modes is autopilot. After the retirement party, the work isn’t finished — it’s just different. Managing a 401(k) in retirement requires just as much—if not more—attention than during your working years.

Markets change. Tax laws change. Your income needs, health status, and family situation may change. What worked when you were 65 might be risky or inefficient at 75\.

Ongoing, proactive management is essential. That doesn’t mean obsessively checking your portfolio every day. But it does mean reviewing your withdrawal strategy, tax position, asset allocation, and account mix on a regular basis. It means treating retirement as a dynamic phase of life that needs active guidance—not autopilot.

### **Rolling Over and Repositioning Your 401(k)**

For many retirees, integration starts with a rollover. When you leave an employer, moving a 401(k) to an IRA can expand your choices and simplify coordination. This isn’t “cashing out”—it’s giving yourself better controls.

Why consider a rollover?

* **Flexibility:** IRAs often offer broader investment menus and cleaner fee structures.  
* **Simplicity:** Fewer accounts make withdrawal sequencing and rebalancing easier.  
* **Control:** Paired with Roth and taxable dollars, an IRA supports tax-aware income design.

Sometimes the move is **partial**: you may keep assets in a 401(k) for a plan-specific benefit (e.g., creditor protection) while shifting a portion to an IRA or Roth for flexibility. There’s no one rule to follow—unless it’s The Mom Rules—and your decision services the larger plan. Important: a rollover is not always the right move. If your current 401(k) has very low costs, strong creditor protections, or useful plan features, keeping some assets there may be smarter. The benefit comes from choosing the structure that improves control and tax coordination for your plan—not from rolling over by default.

That’s why integration matters. You can’t afford to make decisions about income, taxes, investments, or healthcare in isolation. Instead, these need to work together—intentionally. Why this helps in real life: integration prevents one decision from accidentally creating three new problems. Coordinating income, taxes, Medicare, and investments together protects both cash flow and flexibility.

Your 401(k) needs to be managed in concert with:

* **Roth IRA:** tax-free shock absorber for big expenses and future tax risk.

## **Inflation: The Cost Creep You Actually Feel**

One big dark cloud that can slowly creep in and dampen the view… inflation.

Market swings get headlines. Taxes get blamed. But inflation is the quiet force that can shrink your lifestyle one grocery trip at a time—unless your plan stays ahead of it. 

It shows up in small ways: the grocery bill that’s $18 higher than last month, the dinner out that used to be $60 and is now $78 (yep, same meal, same service), the property tax notice that makes you sigh. Same life, same habits—just a little more expensive every time you swipe the card.

That slow drift matters in retirement. Your investments can recover after a market dip; prices almost never go back down. If your income doesn’t have room to grow, the math squeezes you—not overnight, but season after season—until “we’ll take the trip next year” becomes the default answer.

The hardest part about inflation is that it doesn’t feel urgent. Unlike a market crash or a health emergency, it doesn’t demand your attention in the moment. That’s why planning *with* inflation in mind is so important. It gives you the power to stay ahead instead of playing catch-up later.

This section is about building inflation into your plan on purpose: which dollars should grow, which expenses tend to outrun the average, and the simple guardrails that keep your lifestyle steady even as prices climb.

### **Why Inflation Still Matters**

For long stretches of time, inflation wasn’t a hot topic. From the early 1990s through 2020, inflation in the U.S. stayed relatively tame. In fact, from 2009 through 2020, the annual inflation rate came in at or below 2% in eight out of those twelve years. It lulled many people into thinking inflation was more of a textbook concern than a real-world threat (Srinivasan, 2025).

But it hasn’t always been that calm. Anyone who lived through the 1970s and early 80s remembers a very different reality. Between 1969 and 1982, annual inflation was above 5% in roughly twelve of those fourteen years (and above 5.5% in about eleven of fourteen years), peaking at 13.5% in 1980\. That wasn’t just inconvenient—it was brutal.

Fast forward to 2021, and inflation came roaring back. Supply chain issues, labor shortages, and a flood of post-pandemic consumer demand pushed prices higher across the board. By October of that year, the year-over-year inflation rate hit 6.2%—the highest since 1990\. Even the Federal Reserve, after initially calling it “transitory,” had to concede that inflation was back—and no one could say for sure how long it would stay.

The takeaway? Low inflation isn’t a guarantee. And in retirement, you can’t afford to ignore what rising prices can do to your long-term financial security.

### **How Inflation Steals Your Buying Power**

Inflation is sneaky. It doesn’t take your money—it just makes it worth less.

Let’s say you retire with $60,000 a year in income. At 3% annual inflation—a rate many consider modest—your buying power would be cut by almost half over a 25-year retirement. That means the same $60,000 would feel more like $28,700. The numbers in your bank account haven’t changed—but what those dollars *can do* certainly has.

That’s why inflation is especially dangerous for retirees. You’re no longer getting cost-of-living raises from an employer. If your income comes from fixed sources—like a pension, a low-interest annuity, or a conservative investment portfolio—you may not be keeping up.

And that gap between what you have and what things cost? That’s where strain, stress, and difficult decisions can creep in.

### **Fixed Income \= Greater Risk**

Retirees relying heavily on fixed income are especially vulnerable to inflation’s erosion. A pension that pays $3,000 per month today might not feel like enough 10 or 15 years down the road—especially if it lacks a cost-of-living adjustment. The same goes for certain annuities, CDs, or bond-heavy portfolios that aren’t designed to grow with inflation.

In the past, people could put their money in a “safe” CD and earn double-digit interest. Not anymore. Even bonds, once considered a safe haven when stocks declined, no longer behave the way they used to. In today’s low-interest, tech-driven, and highly interconnected market, traditional assumptions don’t always hold.

That’s why relying on yesterday’s strategies to navigate today’s environment can be dangerous. Why this benefits you: without a growth component, “safe” money can quietly reduce your lifestyle over time. Planning for inflation up front helps protect your future spending power and keeps small cost increases from becoming major cutbacks later.

Inflation doesn’t care how “safe” something feels—if it’s not keeping pace, it’s costing you.

### **How to Fight Back—On Purpose**

The good news? Inflation may be inevitable, but its impact on your retirement doesn’t have to be. Here are some of the practical ways we help clients build protection into their plans:

#### **1\. Build inflation into your budget**

Instead of assuming your current income needs will stay flat, add an annual inflation factor into your projections, usually 2–3% per year at a minimum. That way, you’re not surprised down the road when things cost more than you planned for.

Best practice is to factor in individual inflation rates for categories that historically outrun the average—healthcare, property taxes, insurance.

This is the laser-beam part of planning. You’re no longer looking only at what you spend today; you’re looking at which expenses will rise faster, which may stay relatively stable, and which may eventually fall off the plan. That level of detail is what turns a rough budget into a realistic income strategy.

**Good, better, best**

**Good:** Use a simple inflation add-on in your plan (e.g., “We’ll increase our budget 3% a year”). It’s a start.

**Better:** Use one overall inflation rate that’s real and updated each year (not just a default). Now your projections breathe with rising costs.

**Best:** Assign inflation by category—property taxes, groceries, fuel, healthcare, travel—then blend those into your plan. This mirrors real life (healthcare often rises faster than groceries) and can add meaningful dollars over time simply by planning more intentionally.

#### **2\. Include investments with growth potential**

We sometimes see retirees shift their entire portfolio into ultra-conservative positions the moment they stop working. That may feel safe, but over a 25- to 30-year retirement, it can backfire. Investments that don’t at least keep up with inflation don’t just lag—they shrink your real income over time.

That’s why we talk with clients about maintaining a balanced mix of assets that includes some exposure to growth, often through carefully chosen stocks or equity funds. The goal isn’t to chase returns, but to stay ahead of rising costs.

#### **3\. Consider inflation-protected investments**

There are also specific tools designed to help combat inflation, like Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS), which adjust with the Consumer Price Index. Some annuities offer inflation riders, which allow income to increase over time. These tools aren’t right for everyone, but they can play a useful role in the broader strategy.

#### **4\. Regularly revisit your income plan**

Inflation isn’t a one-time problem—it’s an ongoing force. That’s why we emphasize the importance of a “living plan”—one that can flex and evolve as prices rise, markets shift, and life throws curveballs.

Reevaluating your income plan regularly allows you to adjust for inflation before it becomes a crisis. This might mean modifying withdrawals, shifting allocations, or updating your expense assumptions. The key is staying engaged with your plan, rather than treating it as a one-and-done exercise.

### **Mapping Growth Matters**

Growth isn’t a gamble in retirement—it’s a job description. Prices drift up whether markets boom or stall, so a smart plan pairs Protected Income for today’s paycheck with a measured slice of Market-Based Growth tasked explicitly with outpacing inflation, while Roth and taxable dollars give you room to adjust without tax shock.

Keep a clear view by giving yourself a raise on purpose: protect today’s income, keep a measured slice growing for tomorrow, and let your plan coordinate the two.

## **How Hidden Fees Erode Retirement Savings**

Ever wonder how Wall Street’s biggest firms afford their sparkling headquarters, national ad campaigns, private jets, and branded blimps? Who pays for the sleek offices, the TV spots during the big game, or the corporate events on luxury yachts?

In small slices, you do. Not all at once—bit by bit every year.

That’s not said to point fingers. It’s just the reality of how the financial services industry works. Every investor—whether you’ve saved $50,000 or $5 million—is paying *some* type of fee—management, fund expenses, product charges. That part isn’t shocking. As the saying goes, “A laborer is worthy of his hire.”

The problem is when costs are layered, unclear, or higher than necessary—and they repeat every year, compounding against you. Over the course of a 20- to 30-year retirement, even small fees can add up to a big number.

### **Fees You May Not See—But Definitely Feel**

Here’s where some of the most common investment-related fees tend to hide:

* **Management fees**: These are what you pay for professional portfolio oversight, often a clearly stated flat percentage of assets under management—say, 1% annually.  
* **Advisory fees**: These may be layered on top of management fees, especially if you’re working with multiple institutions or platforms. When you’re getting real planning value, it can be worth every penny.  
* **Fund/ETF Expense ratios**: These cover the operating costs of the fund itself and are deducted from your returns before you even see them. Two funds that look identical on paper can have very different ongoing costs. Without a “price scan,” you’d never know which one is quietly skimming more.  
* **Platform/Program Fees**: Custodians and model portfolios sometimes add another thin layer. One layer doesn’t hurt; stack three or four, and your “1%” can quietly become 1.5%–1.8% all-in.  
* **Variable annuity charges**: The Russian nesting dolls of fees: mortality and expense (M\&E) charges, admin fees, internal investment fund costs, riders. Add them up and 2-3%+ a year isn’t unusual. In a flat or down market, that’s a headwind you feel immediately. 

The problem that these fees exist. It’s that layered, opaque, repeat-every-year fees compound against you—especially over a 20–30-year retirement. And you can rely on them to come out whether the market goes up, down, or sideways.

And it’s not just the size of the fee—it’s also *what* you’re paying for.

Sometimes, you’re paying more for branding than for actual value. The logo on the statement, the glitzy commercials, the familiar name—they don’t necessarily translate to better outcomes for *you*. In many cases, higher-fee products are simply that: higher-fee products. They may come with built-in commissions or incentives for advisors that aren’t aligned with your best interests.

I believe the industry needs more transparency. You should know exactly what you’re paying, to whom, and why.

## **See the Cost, Judge the Value**

Paying for good advice is fair. What matters is alignment and transparency—knowing exactly what you’re paying, why, and how your advisor is compensated. In our world, that can include flat advisory fees, internal fund costs, and in some cases, commissions on specific products. None of that is automatically “bad”; it just needs to be clear and worth it.

A **fiduciary** advisor is obligated to put your best interests first and clearly disclose all costs. Some firms (including ours) may be **fee-based**, meaning we charge advisory fees for planning and portfolio management and, in some cases, may also receive **commissions** on certain insurance or annuity products. 

Contrast that with commission-based models, where advisors are compensated for selling specific products—often those with higher internal fees or complex payout structures. Commissions aren’t automatically bad, but they do change incentives.

If you’re not sure which model your advisor uses, ask. The answer may tell you a lot about the type of guidance you’re getting.

The tricky part is the layers you don’t see on the first read. A fund’s expense ratio is only the headline. Beneath it, there can be rider charges, platform/trading fees, pricier share classes, or product costs that repeat every year and quietly pull on returns.

This makes me think of a simple, everyday example. I like to do the grocery shopping occasionally, but I have a bad habit. I walk in hungry. That means I start tossing things into the cart without checking prices—because I like them, or they look good in the moment. I’ve learned that I’m a much smarter shopper when my wife’s with me. She’s like a walking price scanner. She spots the markups. She’ll ask, “Do we really need this one, when that one over there is just as good and half the price?” Without scanning closely and looking at the options, you typically always will overspend.

Portfolios work the same way. Bring a “price scanner” mindset to your money. Check the price before you toss something into your financial cart. Because just like groceries, two similar-looking investments can carry very different price tags. One might be loaded with fees and commissions. The other might be clean, efficient, and just as effective.

When you review an account or sit down with an advisor, use these three questions:

* What’s my all-in annual cost—in % and in dollars? (Advisor fee \+ fund expenses \+ product/platform charges \+ any commissions.)  
* Are there lower-cost ways to accomplish the same goal? Spell out the trade-offs (risk, taxes, liquidity).  
* How are you compensated? Be specific: advisory fee, embedded fund costs, any commission or third-party payment.

Then run the **dollar test**, not just the percent:

“At X% on $Y, I’m paying $\_\_\_\_\_ this year. What’s the 20-year difference versus a \_\_\_% alternative?” 

If the value—planning, tax coordination, rebalancing rules, withdrawal order, clear reporting—exceeds the cost, keep it. If not, swap in the leaner tool and remove the drag.

### **The 1% vs. 1.75% Reality Check**

To keep it apples-to-apples, assume the market delivers a 6% gross return over time. After fees, Sam nets \~5.0% a year; Denise nets \~4.25%.

* Over 20 years, Sam’s $500,000 could grow to roughly **$1.33 million**, while Denise’s lands closer to **$1.14 million**—about **$177,000** less, just from fees.  
* Stretch to 25 years and the spread widens to about **$278,000**.  
* Over 30 years, the difference crosses **$400,000**.

Same markets. Same behavior. Different cost—and a very different ending for Sam and Denise.

That extra $177k–$418k isn’t just a number on a statement. What would that mean to the quality of your retirement? What might it change about the legacy you’re able to leave to loved ones? Where could it bring you peace of mind around health and long-term care?

All of a sudden saving 1% in fees is motivating, isn’t it?

The point isn’t to chase the cheapest thing on the shelf. It’s to **see** the true, all-in cost (in dollars, not just percentages) and decide if the value you’re getting is worth it. Then… kick your feet up and let more of your money do what you saved it to do.

### **Leaner, Smarter Tools**

Good news: Lower cost doesn’t mean lower quality. In fact, lower-cost tools often raise quality by giving you more control and fewer surprises.

Here are a few examples of leaner tools we frequently recommend:

* **Index funds and ETFs**: They track the market instead of trying to outguess it, which usually means lower internal costs and less turnover. Over time, this translates to higher net returns for investors.  
* **Fee-efficient annuities**: Plain-vanilla fixed index annuities (often with low or no explicit fees) can provide downside protection and guaranteed income without the stacked costs common in many variable annuities.

## **Medicare Explained: What’s Actually Covered**

If you’re nearing 65, you’ve probably started getting a steady stream of Medicare mailers—some genuinely helpful, some confusing, and a few that feel like marketing dressed up as advice. And if you’re like most retirees we meet, you’re asking the right questions:

* *What does Medicare actually cover?*  
* *What will it cost me—now and later?*  
* *How do I choose something that fits my life, not just my age?*

Let’s make this simple—because Medicare is a cornerstone of retirement healthcare planning, and the wrong assumption here can create unnecessary stress (and unnecessary expenses) for years.

### **Medicare Is a Framework, Not a Single Plan**

One of the biggest surprises for retirees is this: Medicare doesn’t “just kick in” and cover everything. Since it’s inception in 1965, most workers and employers have funded Medicare through payroll taxes during their working years. It’s not one plan—it’s a set of parts, plus choices you have to make about how you want gaps covered.

That’s why we approach Medicare the same way we approach the Retirement Money Map: with structure. When you understand the lanes, you stop guessing.

**The Medicare Basics: Parts A, B, and D (and the fork in the road)**

We’ll start with Original Medicare—Part A and Part B—then we’ll talk about prescription coverage (Part D) and your two main paths for filling the gaps: Medigap or Medicare Advantage. 

Why this matters to you: Medicare choices are monthly cash-flow decisions, not just insurance decisions. A mismatch here can quietly reduce your spending flexibility every month for years. A strong fit protects both your health access and your retirement lifestyle.

#### **Part A: Hospital Coverage**

Part A helps cover inpatient hospital care, skilled nursing facility care (under specific conditions), hospice, and limited home health services.

Most people don’t pay a premium for Part A if they (or a spouse) paid into the system long enough through work history—but that doesn’t mean it’s “free.” There are deductibles and cost-sharing, and those numbers can be meaningful in a year where you actually need hospital care.

A simple way to think about Part A is: it’s the foundation for hospital coverage—but it’s not a blank check.

#### **Part B: Medical Coverage**

Part B covers the day-to-day “medical” side of care: doctor visits, outpatient services, lab work, imaging, outpatient procedures, physical therapy, and durable medical equipment.

This is the part that catches many people off guard.

I’ve met retirees who assumed Part B worked like employer insurance—until a bill arrived and they realized Medicare generally covers **80%** of approved costs, leaving **20%** as their responsibility. That 20% may feel manageable for routine visits. It can feel very different if you’re facing surgeries, ongoing therapy, or a serious diagnosis. Original Medicare also generally has no annual out-of-pocket maximum for Part B services, which is why supplemental coverage decisions matter.

Part B also has a monthly premium (often deducted from Social Security). And here’s the key: your premium isn’t always the “standard” amount. Higher income can trigger higher Medicare premiums through IRMAA (Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount)—something we’ll discuss in more detail because it’s one of the most common “nobody warned me” surprises.

#### **Part D: Prescription Drug Coverage**

Part D covers prescription medications, but it’s not automatically included with Parts A and B—you enroll separately through a private insurer offering Medicare drug plans.

Part D can be deceptively important. Two plans can look similar on the surface, but your real experience depends on:

* the plan’s drug list (formulary)  
* copays and deductibles  
* whether your medications are considered “preferred” or “non-preferred”

I’ve seen retirees choose a Part D plan based on premium alone—then discover their medication isn’t covered the way they expected. A small decision can quietly become a recurring monthly frustration.

### **Your Two Main Choices for “Gap Coverage”**

Once you have Parts A and B (and usually Part D), you reach the fork in the road. Original Medicare has gaps—so you typically fill them one of two ways:

1. **Medigap (Supplement) \+ Part D**, or  
2. **Medicare Advantage (Part C)**

#### **Option 1: Medigap (Supplemental Insurance)**

Medigap plans are sold by private insurance companies and are designed to help cover the out-of-pocket costs Original Medicare doesn’t—deductibles, copays, coinsurance.

These plans are standardized by letter (Plan G, Plan N, etc.), and while the coverage is standardized, premiums vary by carrier and location. Many people like Medigap because it tends to feel more predictable from a budgeting perspective.

The best Medigap choice depends on your comfort level with monthly premiums versus out-of-pocket variability—and how much you value broad provider access.

One important rule: **you can’t have Medigap and Medicare Advantage at the same time.**

#### **Option 2: Medicare Advantage (Part C)**

Medicare Advantage plans are offered by private insurers approved by Medicare. They typically bundle Parts A and B—and often Part D—into one plan.

Many people are attracted to Medicare Advantage because:

* premiums can be low (sometimes even $0)  
* benefits may include “extras” like dental, vision, or wellness perks

But there are tradeoffs. Advantage plans are usually network-based (HMO/PPO style). That can matter a lot if:

* you travel frequently  
* you want the freedom to see certain specialists  
* your preferred doctors or facilities aren’t in-network

Some retirees love the simplicity. Others only realize the limitations when they try to use the plan in a stressful moment—when a referral is required, a provider is out-of-network, or costs stack up unexpectedly.

One size does *not* fit all. We work closely with Medicare specialists to help clients compare these options and choose the option that supports their lifestyle and peace of mind. 

A simple way to choose: if you value broad provider access and budget predictability, Medigap (supplemental Medicare insurance) often fits better. If lower premiums and bundled convenience matter most—and your doctors and travel patterns fit the network—Medicare Advantage may fit better. The benefit is not picking the “best plan,” but the plan that best supports your real life.

One important caution: if you start with Medicare Advantage and later decide you prefer Medigap, switching back is not always simple. Outside certain protected enrollment periods, you may be subject to medical underwriting—and approval is not guaranteed in many states.

### **IRMAA: The Medicare Surprise Most Retirees Never See Coming**

A couple I’ll call Steve and Joanna did what many retirees are encouraged to do: they completed a large Roth conversion to create more tax-free income later. They expected a higher tax bill that year. What they didn’t expect was a Medicare premium surcharge (IRMAA) two years later.

When they opened their mail, they found a letter explaining their Medicare Part B and Part D premiums were increasing—significantly—because of something they’d never heard of: IRMAA (Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount).

Here’s the catch: Medicare doesn’t base IRMAA on what you earn this year. It looks back two years at your tax return. So a one-time income spike—like a Roth conversion, a large IRA withdrawal, capital gains, or even the sale of a business—can raise your Medicare premiums later, long after the tax year has passed.

The goal isn’t to avoid every surcharge. It’s to avoid getting blindsided. When tax planning and Medicare planning work together, you can often spread big moves over time, coordinate with income thresholds, and make decisions with your eyes open. Here’s the practical payoff of planning ahead: avoiding even a few hundred dollars per month in IRMAA surcharges can preserve thousands per year in retirement cash flow. That is real lifestyle money—trips, gifts, home upgrades, or simply less pressure on your monthly budget.

The not so fun fact: what you do with your income today can affect your healthcare costs two years from now.

### **Avoid the Costly Enrollment Mistakes**

Medicare has rules—and the penalties for missing them can be frustratingly permanent.

You generally have a seven-month window around your 65th birthday to enroll (three months before, the month of, and three months after). Why this is so important: late-enrollment penalties can follow you for life, which means less disposable income every single month. Getting timing right once can protect your budget for decades.

Missing key deadlines—especially for Parts B and D—can lead to late enrollment penalties that follow you.

Timing gets more complex if you’re still working and covered by employer insurance. And one common trap is assuming COBRA (temporary continuation of employer health coverage after leaving a job) works the same way as employer coverage for Medicare timing—it doesn’t always.

This is why we strongly encourage retirees to get Medicare guidance from someone who lives in these rules every day. It’s not about being “smart enough.” It’s about the system being unnecessarily complicated.

### **Tailor Medicare to Your Life**

Medicare shouldn’t be chosen like a default setting. Your choices should reflect your lifestyle, health status, and personal preferences (Lankford, 2024). This is not a “set it and forget it” decision.

Here are just a few of the variables that might affect your Medicare strategy:

* Do you travel often, or spend time in multiple states during the year? Some Advantage plans limit your coverage area.  
* Are you on prescriptions that may not be on every Part D plan’s formulary?  
* Do you see specialists regularly for chronic conditions?  
* Do you want the freedom to see any doctor who accepts Medicare, or are you comfortable with a narrower network?  
* Do you prefer predictability (higher premiums, fewer surprises) or lower premiums with more variable out-of-pocket costs?

Carol, the client who cared for her mother in later years, reminded us how deeply personal healthcare decisions can be. Medicare planning isn’t just about premiums. It’s about dignity, autonomy, and reducing stress on the people you love.

### **The Point of Medicare Planning**

Medicare is too important to treat as an afterthought. The right setup helps you protect your health *and* your budget—and it removes a whole category of “unknowns” from retirement.

Even if you choose well at 65, your needs—and the plans themselves—can change. Drug formularies change. Networks change. Premiums change. Your health changes.

That’s why we recommend reviewing your coverage annually during Medicare Open Enrollment (October 15–December 7). What worked at 65 may not fit at 72—and you deserve a plan that keeps fitting. 

And that’s the real goal of this chapter: not perfection—preparedness.  
So if health changes, you’re not scrambling. You already have a plan.

## **Long-Term Care: What Medicare Won’t Cover and How to Prepare**

When most people picture retirement, they imagine travel, grandkids, hobbies—maybe a slower pace. They don’t picture sitting at the kitchen table trying to coordinate home care, manage medications, or make decisions for a spouse who can no longer do things independently.

And yet for many families, some form of long-term care becomes part of the story. Estimates vary, but a common planning assumption is that 7 in 10 retirees will need some for of long term care in their remaining years. (Marcus, 2022\)

You’ve already seen the price tags. You’ve already heard how quickly family stress can rise. Now let’s talk about what long-term care actually is—and how to plan for it in a way that protects dignity, independence, and family peace.

### **What Long-Term Care Is (and Isn’t)**

Long-term care includes medical and non-medical support for someone who can’t safely perform everyday activities—things like bathing, dressing, eating, or moving around the home. It can happen at home, in assisted living, or in a skilled nursing facility. And it isn’t only a late-80s issue. A fall, stroke, accident, or chronic illness can create care needs much earlier than people expect.

This is where many retirees get surprised: Medicare doesn’t cover most long-term custodial care. Medicare may help with short-term skilled care in very specific circumstances—like rehab after a qualifying hospital stay—or certain medically necessary home health services. But it generally does **not** pay for the type of ongoing help most people need when they can’t manage on their own (U.S. Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services, 2019).

Medicaid does cover long-term care—but it’s needs-based and typically requires spending down assets, with complex eligibility rules and a look-back period.

So for most middle-class and upper-middle-class retirees, the practical reality is this:

If long-term care becomes part of your story, you need a plan for how you’ll fund it.  
Not because you’re pessimistic—because you’re protecting your freedom.

### **Traditional Long-Term Care Insurance: Why It Can Be Hard to Fit Today**

For years, traditional long-term care insurance was the default planning tool. In some cases, it still can make sense. But many families have found it challenging to keep over time.

Here’s why: earlier generations of policies often came with assumptions that didn’t hold—about longevity, claims patterns, and interest rates. As carriers adjusted, many policyholders experienced rising premiums, shifting benefits, or increased complexity.

We’ve seen retirees face frustrating choices: pay significantly more to maintain coverage, adjust benefits to keep premiums manageable, or drop coverage entirely after years of paying in. And because many traditional policies are “use-it-or-lose-it,” some retirees struggle emotionally with the idea of paying for something they may never use.

There’s also the practical side: some policies rely on reimbursement models—pay out of pocket first, then submit receipts—exactly when a family is least equipped to deal with paperwork.

The takeaway isn’t “traditional is bad.” It’s simply that for many families, traditional LTC policies have become harder to fit into a modern retirement plan—especially if flexibility and predictability matter most.

### **More Flexible Planning: Modern Ways to Create a “Care Lane”**

Here’s the good news: there are now approaches that can create a care plan without feeling like you’re paying for something that disappears if you don’t use it. (National Council on Aging, 2024).

In many modern plans, we look for tools that can serve more than one purpose—because that tends to match what people want emotionally:

“If I need care, I have a pool of money to draw from.  
 If I don’t, my family still benefits.”

One common example is life insurance with living benefits—sometimes structured as long-term care riders or accelerated death benefits. In plain language: if you meet certain health criteria (often tied to needing help with daily activities or cognitive impairment), you may be able to access part of the death benefit while you’re living to help pay for care.

Some annuity-based strategies can also be designed to increase available income if care needs arise—creating an additional “buffer” without forcing you to liquidate investments during a downturn.

The point isn’t the product. The point is the planning function:

* creating access to funds if care is needed  
* reducing the odds you’ll have to sell investments at the wrong time  
* and protecting the spouse or family member who will be doing the managing

In other words: tools are just tools. The goal is autonomy. 

And when a strategy supports autonomy, it usually does a few practical things well:

* **Flexibility:** If you never end up needing long-term care, the dollars you set aside can still serve a purpose—supporting retirement income and/or leaving a legacy for loved ones.

* **Simplicity:** Many modern designs are structured to pay benefits once eligibility criteria are met, instead of requiring you to front the costs and chase reimbursements during a stressful season.

* **Efficiency:** In some cases, living benefit riders are built into a policy design or available with minimal additional cost, which can make the solution feel less like a “separate expense” and more like a built-in layer of protection.

The benefit to you is practical: these tools can create a dedicated care funding lane so a health event does not force emotional, high-cost decisions under pressure. They can also protect a spouse from having to liquidate long-term assets at the worst possible time.

### **Self-Funding Needs Structure—or It Can Backfire**

Some retirees say, “We’ll just pay for it ourselves. We have assets.”

That can absolutely be part of the plan. But self-funding works best when it isn’t vague.

Because the challenge isn’t just the cost. It’s the uncertainty:

* Will care last six months—or six years?  
* Will one spouse need care—or both?  
* Will it happen during a strong market—or during a downturn?

And this is where the Sean-and-Jackie-in-2008 scenario matters: relying purely on market-based growth to fund care can create a painful sequence-of-returns problem if care is needed during a market decline.

Self-funding isn’t “wrong.” It just needs guardrails.

Create a clearly labeled “Care Reserve” lane on your Retirement Money Map, keep at least a portion accessible and lower-volatility so you’re not forced to sell in a down market, define what triggers the reserve (like needing help with daily activities or cognitive decline), and write down who will handle the logistics. A little structure here can prevent a health event from turning into a financial and family crisis.

### **What This Means for You**

If you’re wondering whether you need a long-term care strategy, for most retirees the answer is: you need some strategy—because the risk is real and the odds are around 70%. 

Start here:

* **Know what Medicare won’t cover.** Rehab after a hospital stay is different than long-term custodial care. Medicare helps with the first far more than the second.

* **Plan for the human impact, not just the dollars.** Care planning is family planning.

* **Explore flexible approaches.** Modern strategies—including life insurance living benefits or other multi-purpose tools—can create care funding without feeling like “wasted premiums” if care is never needed.

* **Integrate LTC into your retirement plan.** Long-term care shouldn’t be a separate, bolt-on fear. It should be a lane—coordinated with income, taxes, investment strategy, and legacy planning.

We can’t predict the future perfectly.

But it’s reassuring to know that if health changes, you still have options—and your family still has peace.

## **How to Build Health Into Your Retirement Plan**

Healthcare shouldn’t be a side note in retirement planning. It deserves its own lane—because when health changes, spending changes. And having that lane mapped out ahead of time is one of the simplest ways to protect your independence and reduce stress for the people you love.

Here’s a practical way to build it into your plan:

**Start with *your* real numbers.**  
National averages can be useful for context, but your plan should reflect your actual life: your age, current medications, health history, family longevity, and the kind of healthcare you realistically use. The goal isn’t perfection—it’s a realistic starting point you can refine each year.

**Choose coverage on purpose.**  
Lay out your main paths and compare them side by side:

* Original Medicare \+ a supplement (Medigap) \+ Part D, versus  
* Medicare Advantage (Part C), and the Part D options available within your plan

Pay attention to what matters most in real life: premiums, deductibles, provider networks, out-of-pocket exposure, travel flexibility, and how prescriptions are covered.

**Create a dedicated healthcare line item.**  
List the predictable costs (premiums, routine prescriptions, recurring appointments) and leave room for the unpredictable (new diagnoses, procedures, rehab, travel for specialists, equipment). Put it on the map so it’s funded intentionally—not quietly pulled from “fun money” later.

**Plan for long-term care—before you need it.**  
Long-term care planning doesn’t have to be overwhelming. Start by deciding how you’d want care handled if it became necessary: care at home, assisted living, or nursing care. Then decide how you’d want it funded—self-funding with a ring-fenced reserve, insurance-based tools, or a blend. The key is having a written preference so your income plan and legacy plan aren’t thrown into chaos during a health event.

**Add guardrails to any reserve.**  
If you’re self-funding part of the risk, give that reserve structure:

* Keep it separate from discretionary categories like travel  
* Hold at least a portion in steadier, more accessible places so a market dip doesn’t force you into selling at the worst time  
* Define simple “go” triggers in advance—such as a physician’s note, needing help with two activities of daily living, or cognitive impairment that requires supervision—so decisions aren’t made in the middle of a crisis

**Name the roles.**  
One of the kindest things you can do is remove uncertainty from your family. Write down who would:

* call providers or care agencies  
* track bills and insurance paperwork  
* coordinate with doctors  
* communicate with family  
* update the plan

**Review it every year.**  
Medicare plans change. Drug formularies change. Networks change. Your health changes. Your goals change. Revisit your coverage during open enrollment, and review the healthcare lane annually so it stays current and usable.

Because this isn’t just math—it’s peace of mind.

Carol didn’t want her children to become default caregivers by surprise. Many families feel the same way. Building healthcare and long-term care into your Retirement Money Map now is a gift of clarity and calm—for you, and for your family.

## **Planning for Healthcare Is Planning for Freedom**

The best retirement plans start with real life—not just numbers on a page.

So take care of your health on purpose. Keep the routines that keep you strong. Stay engaged with prevention, not because you can control every outcome, but because prevention protects both your body and your budget.

Choose Medicare thoughtfully, not by default—and revisit it each year as your needs change and plans evolve. And if there’s one “quiet fear” this chapter is meant to put a name to, it’s this: the fear of losing independence… and the fear of becoming a burden to the people you love. That fear is especially common for women, who often live longer and may spend many years making health and financial decisions on their own.

The goal isn’t to eliminate every risk. The goal is to remove the panic.

When you plan for long-term care—whether through a clearly defined care reserve, modern tools that can create flexibility, or a blend—you’re doing something deeply protective. You’re giving yourself a calm answer to the big “what if,” so a health event doesn’t force rushed financial decisions or turn your spouse or children into default caregivers.

And here’s the most important takeaway: you can’t separate health from money. A health event can change your income needs, your tax picture, and even your Medicare premiums. When these pieces are coordinated, retirement feels less fragile—and far more manageable.

You don’t have to do this alone. Whether you work with a trusted advisor, a Medicare specialist, or simply follow the steps in this book, fold healthcare—every part of it—into your Retirement Money Map on purpose.

When you do, you trade surprise for confidence. Costs feel planned for, not punishing. Your choices stay yours. And your family gets clarity, not stress.

In retirement, freedom isn’t just free time.  
 It’s knowing that if life throws a curveball, your dignity, independence, and family peace are protected.

From here, the natural next step is legacy. Because what you leave behind isn’t only dollars—it’s the peace you pass along. In the next chapter, we’ll talk about how to protect what you’ve built and shape the impact you want your wealth to have on the people who matter most.

* If you needed care for a longer season (for example, several years), what would your next-best option be if your ideal arrangement wasn’t possible?

* Who are the people you would want involved in care decisions—and what roles would you want them to play?

Now ask one simple question: Does your current financial plan support those preferences—or would your family be forced to “figure it out” under pressure?

# Chapter 8: Leave More Than Money: Building a Legacy That Lasts {#chapter-8:-leave-more-than-money:-building-a-legacy-that-lasts}

*“Your legacy is every life you’ve touched.”* – Maya Angelou

Legacy is how your values keep speaking after you’re gone.

As I shared earlier in the book: there’s no prize for being the wealthiest person in the cemetery. The real goal is simpler, and far more meaningful: to leave the world a little better than we found it—and to make life easier for the people we love.

That’s why this chapter isn’t just about dividing assets. It’s about shaping a legacy that reflects your values, protects your family, and replaces confusion with clarity at an already emotional time. Whether your heart leans toward your children and grandchildren, your church or favorite cause, or simply keeping peace in the family, a clear plan today can become one of the last great acts of love you give.

Because clarity is love.  
Updated beneficiaries are love.  
A trust that keeps your family out of probate can be love.  
A simple sheet that says, “Here’s what I have, here’s where it’s kept, and here’s who to call,” is love.

When someone passes away without clarity, families don’t just grieve—they manage chaos. Not always because anyone is greedy, but because uncertainty creates stress, and stress can create conflict. A thoughtful legacy plan does the opposite: it gives people direction, stability, and peace.

In this chapter, we’ll walk through the essentials—wills, trusts, beneficiary designations, powers of attorney, and healthcare directives—and how they work together as a simple, sturdy system. We’ll also talk about something many families overlook: legacy isn’t only legal; it’s financial and emotional. That includes planning in a way that helps your heirs avoid unnecessary delays, confusion, and—in many cases—unnecessary taxes.

By the end, you’ll know what needs to be in place, the gaps that commonly trip families up, and the simple conversations that turn paperwork into peace of mind. Because your legacy isn’t only what you leave *to* people.

## **How They Remember You (and Why It’s Worth Planning)**

When most people hear the word *legacy*, their minds go straight to money—wills, inheritances, taxes, “who gets what.” That’s part of it. But a real legacy is bigger than numbers on a balance sheet. It’s about how you’re remembered—and whether what you leave behind feels clear and intentional, or confusing and accidental.

I had a client named Lewis—a lifelong bachelor—who didn’t see the point of legacy planning at first. When I asked what he wanted to happen to his money once he was, well, no longer around to spend it, he grinned and said, “I plan to bounce my last check to the undertaker.”

I’ve heard that sentiment from married couples too. Some people work hard their whole lives and see retirement as the season to enjoy the fruit of their labor—without feeling obligated to leave anything behind. And that’s perfectly valid. There’s no guilt and no judgment here. The goal isn’t to preserve every dollar. The goal is to use your money well.

But many people feel a pull to do something more—not because they “have to,” but because they want to.

Some want to make life a little easier for their kids or grandkids. Others want to support a church, a scholarship fund, or a cause that shaped them. Some simply want to be remembered with love and gratitude—not with confusion, conflict, and a mess their family has to untangle in the middle of grief.

That’s where intentional legacy planning comes in.

### **Net Worth vs. Net Impact**

When we sit down with families, we don’t start with spreadsheets. We start with a question:

*What do you want your legacy to be?*

Not just in dollars and documents—in impact.

Your net worth is what you owned.  
Your net impact is what your life—and your planning—made possible for others.

Legacy is the emotional footprint you leave behind. It’s the wisdom you pass on to your children, the memories you create with your grandchildren, the handwritten note they find years later in your Bible or your recipe box. It’s the example you set—through generosity, kindness, and integrity—that helps guide the next generation.

And you don’t need a fortune to leave a meaningful legacy.

Some families will never fund a building or start a foundation. But their children remember Sunday pancakes. They remember generosity without fanfare. They remember how you treated people, how you handled hard seasons, how you served your community, how you prayed, how you forgave. Those are the things that shape a family long after money is spent.

Yes—there are practical pieces we’ll cover in this chapter: keeping beneficiaries current, avoiding probate delays where possible, minimizing taxes, and making sure your wishes are actually carried out. But the heart of legacy planning is purpose.

*What kind of life do you want your money to support?*  
*What values do you want living on in your family?*  
*What causes do you want standing after you’re gone?*

And here’s the part many people miss:

Say it out loud.

A plan without communication can still create confusion. But when your loved ones understand the “why” behind the “what,” your plan feels like a gift—not a puzzle.

Tell your children why the lake cabin matters. Tell them why a portion goes to your church or a scholarship fund. Tell them what you hope your money *does*, not just where it goes. Sometimes that simple conversation preserves family harmony more effectively than any legal clause ever could.

### **Accidental vs. Intentional Legacies**

Whether you plan or not, you will leave a legacy.

The question is whether it’s intentional—or accidental.

An accidental legacy often looks like this: loved ones grieving and also scrambling—searching for documents, guessing at wishes, sorting out accounts, trying to be fair, and sometimes disagreeing along the way. Not because anyone is bad, but because uncertainty creates stress, and stress can create conflict.

An intentional legacy looks different. It feels like direction. It feels like steadiness. It feels like:

* “Here’s what I wanted.”  
* “Here’s where everything is.”  
* “Here’s who to call.”  
* “And here’s why I made these choices.”

That kind of clarity is one of the last great acts of love.

Because your true legacy isn’t only what you leave *to* people.

It’s what you leave *in* them—the story they tell when they say your name, the peace your preparation gave them, and the difference your life made.

## **Documents That Protect Your Legacy—and Your Family**

If legacy is about more than just money, then one of the most lasting gifts you can leave behind is clarity. Updated beneficiaries are love. A simple plan that keeps your family out of chaos is love.

This part of legacy planning isn’t glamorous—but it may be the most caring thing you do, because it spares your loved ones from having to “figure it out” while they’re grieving.

### **The Paper Trail No One Wants to Chase**

When someone passes away—even when it’s expected—grief often comes hand in hand with confusion.

Where is the will?  
Which accounts existed?  
Who is listed as beneficiary on that old IRA?  
Who do we call first?

We’ve seen families overwhelmed, not just by sadness, but by the scavenger hunt. And it’s rarely because anyone did something “wrong.” It’s usually because the documents were scattered, outdated, or never organized in a way someone else could use.

That’s why we encourage people to create a simple “legacy roadmap”—a single place that lists what you have, where it’s held, and who to contact. It doesn’t need to be fancy. It just needs to exist. Think: financial accounts, insurance policies, real estate details, passwords, trusted contacts, and even the location of your safe deposit box key. If you use digital accounts, it also means a secure plan for access.

Because you may know where everything is. But if your family doesn’t, it can turn an already difficult season into a logistical nightmare.

I’ve heard it more than once: “We love you, Dad—but why is it so hard to find your will? He did have a will… right?”

### **The Legal Must-Haves**

I’m not an attorney (and don’t play one on TV), so I won’t try to give you legal advice. But I will tell you this: there are a handful of foundational documents almost every family should review, update, and keep accessible. (Curtis, 2022).

Here are the big ones:

#### **1\. A Will**

A will outlines who receives what when you’re gone. Without one, the state decides—and that process can be slow, public, and expensive. Even with a will, probate may still be involved, but without one, you’re almost guaranteeing extra friction for the people you love.

And here's something that hits close to home: After my dad passed, my mom found herself approached by people claiming he owed them money—people she’d never even heard of. Why? Because his will, once filed with the probate court, became public record. Anyone could see what he owned and take a shot at claiming a piece. Unfortunately, grief doesn’t always bring out the best in others, and it was a hard lesson in why privacy and structure matter.

#### **2\. Living Trust (when appropriate)**

For some families, a living trust can reduce probate delays, increase privacy, and make distribution smoother. The key phrase is “when appropriate”—a trust isn’t necessary for everyone, and it’s only effective if it’s set up properly and actually funded the right way.

A good estate attorney can help you decide whether a trust fits your situation and goals.

#### **3\. Healthcare Proxy and Financial Power of Attorney (POA)**

A healthcare proxy (or medical power of attorney) names someone to make medical decisions if you can’t. A financial power of attorney allows someone to manage financial matters—pay bills, handle accounts, and keep life running—if you’re incapacitated.

One important note: powers of attorney apply while you’re living. They generally end when you pass away. That’s why your plan needs to address both seasons: while you’re here and after you’re gone.

#### **4\. Beneficiary Designations**

Many assets—retirement accounts, annuities, and life insurance—pass by beneficiary designation, often outside of probate. That’s efficient… unless the designations are outdated.

Beneficiary updates prevent unintended recipients. 

I’ve seen a situation where a surviving spouse discovered an ex-spouse was still listed as the beneficiary on a 401(k). It’s a brutal surprise at a terrible time—and it can create legal and emotional fallout that was completely avoidable.

Names change. Families change. Life changes. Review beneficiaries at least annually and anytime there’s a major life event.

#### **5\. Transfer-on-Death (TOD) and Pay-on-Death (POD) Designations**

Many bank and brokerage accounts, and in some states certain property titles, can include “pay on death” or “transfer on death” designations. These can help assets pass directly to the people you choose without adding unnecessary probate steps. They’re often simple to set up, helping speed transfers and reduce friction.

### **Probate: Why It Matters (Even for “Normal” Families)**

Probate is the legal process that validates a will, settles debts, and distributes assets. The challenge is that probate can be slow, costly, and public. And it’s not just a “wealthy family problem.” Even modest estates can face delays—especially if documents are unclear, accounts aren’t organized, or family members disagree.

Often, good planning can reduce probate friction: a trust when appropriate, updated beneficiaries, and a clear organizational system.

### **Avoiding Family Fallout Starts Now**

When someone passes without clear instructions, the pain isn’t only legal—it’s emotional. Misunderstandings can become conflict. Siblings can stop speaking. Old wounds can reopen. Not because anyone wanted that, but because uncertainty creates pressure—and pressure can bring out the worst in people.

But when your affairs are in order, it’s like handing your loved ones a roadmap:

“Here’s what I wanted. Here’s where everything is. Here’s who to call. And here’s why I made these choices.”

That kind of clarity is one of the last—and greatest—acts of love you can offer.

And if you’ve been putting this off because it feels uncomfortable, you’re not alone. Estate planning is easy to delay because it forces us to look at something we’d rather not think about.

But the work you do now—just a few focused hours—can spare your family months (or years) of stress later.

You don’t have to do it alone. A trusted fiduciary or independent advisor can help you coordinate with qualified estate attorneys, organize accounts, and create a simple system to keep everything current.

Start with progress, not perfection. Your family will feel the difference.

## **Modern Life Insurance: When It Fits, It Can Strengthen a Legacy**

In all my years of planning, I’ve never heard a widow say, “I wish we had *less* support.” When life changes in an instant, what families usually wish for is breathing room—time to grieve without immediately panicking about money.

That said, this isn’t a sales pitch for life insurance. I have a complicated relationship with the industry—and it’s personal.

As I shared earlier, my father died suddenly at 49, just months after a policy he’d paid into for 23 years quietly lapsed. Like many policies of its kind, it was designed in a way that allowed cash value to cover premiums for a period of time—until the rising internal costs eventually overtook it. Notices came in the mail. The original agent was long gone. And the coverage my parents thought they had… was gone right before we needed it most. When my dad passed, my mom was left with no support from the coverage they had counted on. 

That experience shaped the way I look at life insurance today—not with fear, and not with blind enthusiasm, but with one clear lesson:

Structure matters. And stewardship matters.

A policy that’s designed poorly or left on autopilot can create disappointment. But a policy that’s chosen thoughtfully, structured conservatively, and reviewed over time can be one of the cleanest ways to deliver support—exactly when it’s needed.

And in legacy planning, that’s the point. Not bells and whistles. Not shiny illustrations. Just dependable dollars that create stability for the people you love.

### **Life Insurance That Works While You’re Living, Too**

Most people think of life insurance as a death benefit—and that’s still the core function. But some modern policies can also provide flexibility while you’re living, especially when they include living benefit features that may help in a health event.

This connects directly to Chapter 7: one of the hardest legacy risks is that long-term care costs can unintentionally consume the inheritance you hoped to leave. Certain policies are designed so that if care is needed, benefits may be accessed during life—and if care isn’t needed, something still passes on to your family or a cause you care about.

That’s why many retirees find this concept emotionally appealing:

“If I need it, it supports my dignity.  
 If I don’t, it supports my family.”

It’s not right for everyone. But it’s worth understanding as an option—especially when the goal is reducing future stress, not maximizing an account statement.

### **You Deserve Guidance, Not Pressure**

If you’ve ever had a pushy experience with insurance, you’re not alone. The industry has earned some skepticism. That’s why the standard here should be simple: **no pressure, clear disclosure, and real review.**

A few guardrails we encourage families to insist on:

* **Transparent costs and tradeoffs.** Not just the premium—how the policy behaves over time.  
* **Conservative expectations.** If a policy only works in a best-case scenario, it isn’t a plan.  
* **Flexibility.** Life changes; your plan should be able to adjust.  
* **Ongoing stewardship.** Policies shouldn’t be “set and forget.” They should be reviewed periodically to ensure they’re still doing what you intended.  
* **Second opinions when needed.** Especially if something feels unclear or overly complex.

Done right, insurance should feel like a tool—not a trap.

## **Leaving Support, Not a Tax Problem**

For many families, the biggest surprise in legacy planning isn’t estate tax.

It’s income tax.

Specifically, what happens when retirement accounts are passed to the next generation.

In 2019, Congress passed the SECURE Act, which significantly changed how inherited retirement accounts are handled. One of its biggest impacts was eliminating the long-standing “stretch IRA” strategy for most non-spouse beneficiaries.

For years, adult children who inherited a traditional IRA could “stretch” withdrawals over their lifetime—spreading the tax impact out slowly. Today, for most non-spouse beneficiaries, inherited retirement accounts must be fully withdrawn within ten years. In many cases, beneficiaries may also need to take annual distributions during years 1–9, depending on when the original owner died.

Here’s why that matters.

Those ten years often fall right in the middle of your children’s highest-earning seasons—when they’re building careers, raising families, and already paying the highest marginal tax rates of their lives. Add large required withdrawals on top of that, and what was meant to be a blessing can quickly feel like a burden.

I’ve had families say, “We worked our whole lives to save this—and now a huge portion of it is going straight to the IRS.”

That’s not what most parents intend.

This is where legacy planning shifts from simply *leaving assets* to *leaving support*. The goal isn’t to maximize what you pass on at all costs. It’s to leave something that’s easier to receive, easier to manage, and less stressful to inherit.

When tax-smart coordination is part of the plan, your family may keep more of what you intended, receive it with less complexity, and avoid a compressed tax hit during already stressful years.

It looks at questions like:

* Should some assets be repositioned into accounts that are easier for heirs to manage?

## **What Fiduciary Really Means**

Let’s start with a simple definition: a fiduciary is someone who is obligated to put *your* best interest at the center of the advice they give.

That might sound like common sense. Most people assume, “If I’m hiring an advisor, of course they’re on my side.” And many advisors truly care about their clients. The difference is that not every financial professional is held to the same standard of care.

Some professionals operate under what’s often called a suitability standard. In simple terms, that means a recommendation can be *acceptable* for your situation without necessarily being the best fit when you weigh costs, risks, trade-offs, and long-term impact. It isn’t always “bad advice.” It’s just not always fully optimized for you.

A fiduciary standard raises the bar. It means the advice should be grounded in your goals, your full financial picture, and an honest explanation of alternatives—so you understand what you’re choosing and why. This standard affects what gets recommended, what you pay, and how clearly conflicts are disclosed. 

Over a long retirement, that can materially change net outcomes and your confidence in the advice you’re following.

And since this matters, let me say one more thing plainly: being a fiduciary isn’t about a specific fee structure. It’s about how recommendations are made, how conflicts are handled, and how transparent the advisor is with you. For example, at Capital Wealth, we are fee-based, charging advisory fees for investment management, and in some cases we also implement solutions where commissions may apply. When that happens, it should be clearly disclosed—and the recommendation should still stand on the same foundation: what serves you best.

This is big. You’re allowed to not understand Medicare. Or RMDs. Or investment fees. Or how taxes will work once paychecks stop. A good planning relationship should feel like a place where questions are welcomed and explanations are patient—without judgment and without jargon.

### **Remember What the First Meeting Should Feel Like**

A good first meeting shouldn’t feel like a presentation. It should feel like a conversation—where someone listens carefully, helps you sort what matters, and starts bringing the pieces into one clear picture.

You don’t have to show up with everything perfect.  
Just show up willing to be honest about what you want—and what you’re tired of carrying.

That’s enough to begin.

# Closing Note: Enjoy the Life You Deserve {#closing-note:-enjoy-the-life-you-deserve}

Well… here we are. You made it all the way through.

We’ve climbed a few mountains together. We’ve dropped some anchors when things felt choppy. And along the way, we walked through what retirement really looks like in real life—not the glossy brochure version. The questions. The risks. The emotional rollercoaster. The moments that can make you feel confident… and the ones that can make you wonder if you’re missing something.

We’ve covered the hard stuff: income, taxes, fees, healthcare, inflation, market headlines, and the quiet fears most people don’t say out loud.

But we’ve also covered the good stuff—the kind that makes all this worth doing. Freedom. Peace of mind. Time with the people you love. The ability to stop second-guessing and start living.

Here’s the beautiful part: retirement isn’t just a financial event. It’s a human one. It’s a season you get to shape with intention—where the goal isn’t to “get it perfect,” but to feel steady enough to actually enjoy the life you deserve.

If you take one thing from this book, let it be this: retiring well isn’t a math test. It’s a life decision. And the goal isn’t a perfect spreadsheet—it’s clarity. It’s waking up without that low-grade worry in the background. It’s knowing the plan is doing its job, so you can finally be present for the life you worked so hard to build.

You’ve spent years doing what responsible people do. You saved. You sacrificed. You showed up. Somewhere along the way, it’s easy to start living as if peace is something you earn later—after the next milestone, the next market cycle, the next “just in case.” But this season of life is different. You’ve earned the right to move from worry to presence. And a real plan is what makes that possible.

That’s what the Retirement Money Map is really about. Not predicting the future. Not trying to control every variable. It’s about giving yourself room to live well inside whatever the future brings. It’s about taking all the moving parts—income, investments, taxes, healthcare, inflation, and legacy—and turning them into one coordinated framework that supports your life instead of consuming it.

Because when those pieces work together, retirement stops feeling like a cliff and starts feeling like solid ground.

* Income becomes something you understand and can count on—not something you hope works out.  
* Investments stop being a constant emotional headline and start being a tool that serves your goals and your temperament.  
* Taxes become a strategy, not a surprise—so you keep more of what you’ve earned and avoid leaving your family avoidable burdens.  
* Healthcare and longevity are planned for with guardrails—so a health event doesn’t become a financial crisis.  
* Inflation is acknowledged and built into the structure—so your lifestyle isn’t quietly chipped away at over time.  
* Legacy becomes more than documents—it becomes clarity, communication, and peace passed forward.  
* And partnership becomes what it was always meant to be: a steady relationship that helps you make decisions with confidence as life changes.

None of this is abstract. It’s the difference between a retirement you endure and a retirement you enjoy.

I’ve seen firsthand what happens when “the paperwork exists,” but the clarity doesn’t. When my dad passed, my mom had folders. She had documents. But she didn’t have the kind of shared understanding that makes those documents feel like a roadmap. In a season where she needed steadiness most, she was left piecing things together while carrying grief. It’s one of the reasons I believe a plan has to be more than numbers and signatures. It has to be understood. It has to be communicated. It has to be lived.

And that’s where many people reach a quiet, honest conclusion: not “I can’t do this,” but “I don’t want to carry this alone.” Not because they’re incapable—but because they want their home to feel peaceful. They want to spare their spouse and children confusion. They want decisions to feel clear instead of heavy. They want to enjoy the life they’ve earned without constantly second-guessing every move.

Before you close these pages, let me ask you one simple question:

*What does retirement actually mean to you?*

Not in theory. Not in someone else’s version.  
In your life.

Is it freedom? Time? Relief? Being present? Creating memories?  
Is it protecting a spouse? Staying independent? Giving generously?  
Is it finally exhaling after years of responsibility?

That question matters more than any rate of return.  
Because when you’re clear on what you want retirement to *feel* like, the plan stops being abstract—and starts becoming a guide.

So here’s what I hope you do next—gently, without pressure, at your pace:

Revisit your plan with fresh eyes. Bring the people you love into the conversation. Write down what matters most—and why. Make sure the strategy you’ve built reflects the life you actually want to live.

Then—live it.

Say yes to the trip while you can enjoy it. Say yes to the Tuesday ballgame, the quiet coffee, the long walk, the weekend away, the family dinner that turns into two hours of laughter. Give in ways that feel meaningful to you. Protect your health. Protect your relationships. Those are the real returns.

You’ve built something meaningful. Now let your planning do what it was always meant to do: buy you back the freedom to be present.

This is your time.

Retire *right*. Retire *now*.

Enjoy the life you deserve.
